\title{Complete CALM: A Universal Criterion for Coordination-Freedom}

\author{Joseph M. Hellerstein}
\affiliation{%
  \institution{UC Berkeley \& Amazon Web Services}
  \city{Berkeley, CA}
  \country{USA}
}

\begin{abstract}
  Coordination is a common bottleneck in distributed and database systems.
  It is widely understood that monotonically growing outputs require no
  coordination: CRDTs enforce this by construction, and the CALM theorem
  proves it bidirectionally for Datalog.
  But these results live in walled gardens---each tied to a specific
  computational model (relational transducers, semilattices).
  This model-specificity has two consequences.
  First, the results do not transfer across models: a coordination
  boundary proved for Datalog says nothing about transactional isolation
  specifications or replicated objects.
  Second, and more fundamentally, prior frameworks cannot verify that a
  system is \emph{well-coordinated}---that coordination already applied
  suffices and no more is needed---because each inspects program
  internals where non-monotone logic remains even after coordination.

  We prove \emph{Complete CALM}: a specification is well-coordinated if
  and only if its observable outcomes are monotone.
  The criterion operates on output semantics over Lamport histories with
  any refinement order, independent of programming language or
  computational model---solving the generality problem.
  As a natural consequence, it passes the litmus test: a non-monotone
  program composed with a coordination protocol produces monotone
  output, and Complete CALM verifies this.
  We prove a universal construction showing that establishing membership
  suffices to make any specification well-coordinated.
  We instantiate the criterion on transactional isolation levels,
  invariant confluence, and CRDTs, recovering known coordination
  boundaries as instances of a single semantic test.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% ================================================================

Coordination---the use of protocols such as consensus, atomic commit,
and distributed locking to enforce agreement among nodes---is a
recurring cost in distributed systems.
It entails latency (nodes must wait for protocol rounds), complexity
(correct implementations are notoriously subtle), and fragility
(coordination protocols are typically the first component to fail under
partitions or load).
These costs motivate a fundamental question: when is coordination
\emph{necessary}, and when can it be safely avoided?

A productive line of work has addressed this question by connecting
coordination-freedom to monotonicity.
CRDTs~\cite{shapiro2011crdt} enforce monotonicity by construction
(inflationary updates on a join-semilattice).
The CALM theorem~\cite{hellerstein2010declarative,ameloot2013relational,ameloot2015weaker}
proves the connection bidirectionally for Datalog: a program admits
coordination-free distributed evaluation iff it is monotone.
Baccaert and Ketsman~\cite{baccaert2026spectrum} extend the result to
non-deterministic behaviors with parameterized system knowledge.

These results live in walled gardens.
Each is stated within a specific computational model---relational
transducers, join-semilattices, distributed instances---and each
diagnoses coordination requirements by analyzing the \emph{program} or
\emph{problem function} that produces the output.
This model-specificity has two consequences.

\emph{(a) Lack of generality.}
To use CALM as a diagnostic, one must encode the system in relational
transducers; CRDTs require a lattice encoding.
Encodings introduce complications: e.g., different Datalog programs for the same system
can have different monotonicity propertie. The diagnosis may depend more on
the encoding than the original system.
More fundamentally, many coordination questions are not about programs
at all---they concern \emph{specifications}: is serializability
inherently coordination-requiring?
Does invariant confluence characterize coordination-free integrity
enforcement?
Such questions are naturally stated at the level of specifications,
not as properties of executable programs in a particular language.

\emph{(b) Failure of a key litmus test.}
No prior framework can verify that a system is
\emph{well-coordinated}---that coordination already applied suffices and
no more is needed.
Consider a non-monotone Datalog program $P^{\neg}$ that CALM correctly
identifies as requiring coordination.
Now consider the program $P_{\mathit{lin}}(P^{\neg})$, in which all
inputs and messages to $P^{\neg}$ first pass through a linearization
layer $P_{\mathit{lin}}$ that totally orders them and broadcasts the
result to each replica of $P^{\neg}$.
The replicas will be consistent: each sees the same ordered input and
produces the same output.
The overall system is well-coordinated---no further coordination is
needed beyond what $P_{\mathit{lin}}$ provides.
But \emph{both} $P^{\neg}$ and $P_{\mathit{lin}}$ are non-monotone
Datalog programs ($P_{\mathit{lin}}$ establishes a total order, which
requires negation: adjacency $\mathit{next}(\ell_k, \ell_{k+1})$
demands $\neg\exists j \neq k{+}1.\; \mathit{next}(\ell_k, j)$).
CALM, applied to the composed program text, sees non-monotone operators
throughout and cannot distinguish ``requires coordination locally'' from
``well-coordinated \emph{in toto}.''\!

\medskip
Our key modeling decision is to characterize coordination in terms of
\emph{specifications}---relations from histories to sets of admissible
outcomes---rather than programs.
No program is required: the criterion applies directly to theoretical
properties such as isolation levels, consistency models, and invariant
conditions.
When a program \emph{is} present, we test its output specification, not
its syntax.

We prove \emph{Complete CALM}: a specification is well-coordinated if
and only if its observable outcomes are \emph{monotone}---as
histories grow causally, admissible outcomes refine rather than
contradict earlier observations.
Monotonicity is the exact semantic characterization of the
coordination boundary.
Because it operates on specifications rather than programs, it solves
problem~(a) (no encoding into any language needed; applicable to
theoretical properties directly) and passes litmus test~(b) (non-monotone
program internals are invisible to the output-level test) as a natural
consequence.
Applied to the example above: the output specification of
$P_{\mathit{lin}}(P^{\neg})$---command results ordered by prefix
extension---is monotone.
Complete CALM verifies that the system is well-coordinated.
CALM and its successors cannot, because they inspect
program text rather than output semantics.

Coordination-freedom---the property that CALM, CRDTs, and related work
characterize---is the special case of well-coordination where no
coordination has been applied at all.
Complete CALM subsumes this: a coordination-free specification is one
that is well-coordinated from the start (monotone without any
prior resolution).
The broader category includes systems where coordination has been
applied at some layer, and the resolved output requires no more.

We instantiate the criterion on specifications central to data
management.
The HAT/non-HAT boundary for transactional isolation
levels~\cite{bailis2013hat}, the $I$-confluence condition for
invariant-preserving replication~\cite{bailis2014coordination}, and the
semilattice discipline of CRDTs~\cite{shapiro2011crdt} all emerge as
instances of the same semantic test.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item The \emph{Complete CALM theorem}: monotonicity is the
        exact semantic characterization of well-coordination, aligning
        the informal intuition with a precise model-independent test
        (Section~\ref{sec:complete-calm}).
  \item A \emph{strict separation} from all prior CALM-like results:
        Complete CALM verifies that coordination correctly discharges
        non-monotone specifications---the litmus test that prior
        frameworks~\cite{ameloot2013relational,baccaert2026spectrum}
        fail (Section~\ref{sec:beyond-calm}).
  \item A \emph{universal construction}: establishing membership (one
        round of distributed coordination) suffices to reduce any
        specification to well-coordinated local evaluation
        (Section~\ref{sec:universal-construction}).
  \item \emph{Uniform recovery} of known coordination boundaries for
        isolation levels, invariant confluence, and CRDTs as instances of
        monotonicity (Section~\ref{sec:applications}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Running Example: A Replicated Register}
\label{sec:example}
% ================================================================

Consider a single logical register, replicated at two nodes $p$ and $q$,
supporting read and write operations.
We define two specifications over the \emph{same} histories that differ
only in which outcomes they admit.

\paragraph{Histories.}
A history records invocations and responses of reads and writes at each
node, together with message sends and receives that propagate updates.
Following the standard model for
linearizability~\cite{herlihy1990linearizability}, each operation
consists of an \emph{invocation} event and a \emph{response} event;
an operation is \emph{complete} when both are present.
Events are partially ordered by happens-before: program order within each
node, and send-before-receive for each message.
Neither fairness nor delivery guarantees are assumed.

\paragraph{Two specifications.}
Both specifications use the same outcome domain: sequences of completed
operations annotated with return values, ordered by \emph{prefix extension}
($o_1 \Ord o_2$ iff $o_1$ is a prefix of $o_2$).
They differ in which sequences are admissible:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item $\Spec_{\mathit{ec}}$ (eventual consistency): a completed read
        at node $q$ may return the initial value or any value written in
        the causal past of that read's invocation.
        Multiple return values are admissible at any prefix.
  \item $\Spec_{\mathit{lin}}$ (linearizability): the sequence of
        completed operations must admit a linearization respecting
        real-time order (invocation-to-response intervals).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{The witness.}
Let history $H_1$ contain: a completed write $w(1)$ at node $p$
(both invocation and response present), a read invocation
$\mathit{inv}(r)$ at node $q$ (response not yet present), and a
propagation message $s$ from $p$ (sent but not yet received at $q$).
The write and read are concurrent: neither's response precedes the
other's invocation.
At $H_1$, both $\langle r \mapsto 0 \rangle$ and
$\langle r \mapsto 1 \rangle$ are admissible completions under
$\Spec_{\mathit{lin}}$: both linearizations are valid.

Now extend $H_1$ to $H_2$ by adding the receive of $s$ at $q$, followed
by the read response $\mathit{resp}(r, 1)$.
This is a valid future: both new events are causally \emph{after}
existing events (the receive follows the send; the response follows the
invocation), so no new predecessor of an existing event is introduced.
In $H_2$, the write's response precedes the read's response in real
time, so every linearization places $w$ before $r$:
$\Obs_{\mathit{lin}}(H_2)$ contains only sequences where $r$ returns $1$.
The earlier admissible outcome $\langle r \mapsto 0 \rangle$ has no
prefix-extension in $\Obs_{\mathit{lin}}(H_2)$---it is
\emph{future-inconsistent}.

Under $\Spec_{\mathit{ec}}$, the same extension poses no problem:
a read returning $0$ is consistent with having been served from a stale
replica before the propagation message arrived.
Every outcome admitted at a prefix has a refinement at every extension.

This single witness separates the two specifications:
$\Spec_{\mathit{ec}}$ is monotone; $\Spec_{\mathit{lin}}$ is not.
As we will show, this is exactly the boundary between coordination-free
and coordination-requiring behavior.
Note that the non-monotonicity arises from \emph{concurrency} (the read
and write are concurrent), not from distribution per se---the same
phenomenon occurs with concurrent threads on a single machine.
Replication across nodes makes the coordination cost \emph{operational}
(messages, partitions), but the \emph{need} for coordination is a
property of the specification under concurrency.

\section{Framework}
\label{sec:model}
% ================================================================

We model concurrent executions as Lamport histories and define
specifications, implementations, and coordination-freedom in terms of
observable outcomes.
The goal is to isolate a single semantic question: when does a
specification force an implementation to suppress otherwise-admissible
futures?

The framework applies to any concurrent setting---distributed or local.
Coordination has costs in both: locking and abort in a local database,
latency and partition vulnerability in a distributed system.
The criterion characterizes when these costs are \emph{intrinsic} to the
specification (unavoidable under any implementation) versus
\emph{incidental} (artifacts of a particular implementation strategy).
The universal construction (Section~
ef{sec:universal-construction})
addresses the distributed case specifically, showing that one round of
distributed coordination suffices to eliminate all further
cross-node costs.

We build toward the criterion in three steps: first the execution model
(histories and their causal growth), then the specification language
(what outcomes are admissible and how they relate), and finally the
implementation model (what it means to be coordination-free).

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Histories and Futures}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

We represent concurrent executions as partially ordered sets of events,
following Lamport~\cite{lamport1978time}.
The partial order captures causal precedence; concurrent events have no
ordering.

\begin{definition}[Event universe]
  A specification fixes pairwise disjoint sets of events:
  $E_{\mathit{in}}$ (external inputs),
  $E_{\mathit{int}}$ (internal computation),
  $E_{\mathit{send}}$ (message sends),
  $E_{\mathit{recv}}$ (message receives).
\end{definition}

\noindent
The disjoint sets distinguish interface activity from internal
computation and message-passing structure.
In the register example, $E_{\mathit{in}}$ contains client read/write
invocations; $E_{\mathit{int}}$ contains local state updates;
$E_{\mathit{send}}$ and $E_{\mathit{recv}}$ contain inter-replica
propagation messages.

\begin{definition}[History]
  \label{def:history}
  A \emph{history} is a pair $H = (E, \rightarrow)$ where
  $E \subseteq E_{\mathit{in}} \cup E_{\mathit{int}} \cup
  E_{\mathit{send}} \cup E_{\mathit{recv}}$,
  $\rightarrow$ is a strict partial order on $E$, and
  for every receive $r \in E$ there exists a unique send
  $s \in E$ with $s \rightarrow r$ (each send precedes at most one
  receive).
  A history is \emph{well-formed} if it satisfies these constraints.
  We write $\Hist$ for the set of all well-formed histories over a fixed
  event universe.
\end{definition}

\noindent
A history need not be complete: it may represent a prefix with pending
operations or in-flight messages.
Messages may be delayed, reordered, or lost (a send without a matching
receive denotes a lost or indefinitely delayed message).
We impose no fairness or progress assumptions.

\begin{example}[Register history $H_1$]
  \label{ex:h1-formal}
  The history $H_1$ from Section~\ref{sec:example} contains:
  a write-invocation event $w$ at node $p$ (in $E_{\mathit{in}}$),
  a read-invocation event $r$ at node $q$ (in $E_{\mathit{in}}$),
  and a send event $s$ at $p$ propagating the write (in
  $E_{\mathit{send}}$), with $w \rightarrow s$.
  No receive event for $s$ appears in $H_1$---the message is in flight.
  Since $w$ and $r$ share no causal path, they are concurrent.
\end{example}

As a distributed execution unfolds, new events occur.
A \emph{future} of a history preserves all existing events and their
causal order, adding only causally later events:

\begin{definition}[Future]
  \label{def:future}
  For $H_1 = (E_1, \rightarrow_1)$ and $H_2 = (E_2, \rightarrow_2)$,
  we write $H_1 \hext H_2$ (\emph{$H_2$ is a future of $H_1$}) if:
  \begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\roman*)]
    \item $E_1 \subseteq E_2$,
    \item $\rightarrow_1 = \rightarrow_2 \cap (E_1 \times E_1)$, and
    \item $E_1$ is downward-closed under $\rightarrow_2$: if
          $e \in E_1$ and $e' \rightarrow_2 e$, then $e' \in E_1$.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\noindent
Condition (iii) is the key constraint: a future can add causally
\emph{later} events but cannot insert new predecessors of existing ones.
The past is fixed; only the future is open.

\begin{example}[Extending $H_1$ to $H_2$]
  \label{ex:h2-formal}
  The extension $H_2$ from Section~\ref{sec:example} adds a receive
  event $r_s$ at node $q$ (the delivery of message $s$), with
  $s \rightarrow r_s$ and $r_s \rightarrow r$.
  This is a valid future: $r_s$ is causally later than $s$ (which is
  already in $H_1$), and the new edge $r_s \rightarrow r$ does not
  introduce a predecessor of any event already in $H_1$ (since $r_s$
  itself is new).
  The result is that $w \rightarrow r$ holds in $H_2$ (transitively
  through $s$ and $r_s$), making the write causally prior to the read.
\end{example}

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Specifications}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

A specification says which outcomes are admissible at each history and
how outcomes relate to one another under refinement.
It is a purely semantic object: it constrains what may be observed, not
how executions unfold.

\begin{definition}[Specification]
  \label{def:spec}
  A \emph{specification} is a triple $\Spec = (E, \Obs, \Ord)$ where:
  \begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item $E$ is an event universe fixing the space of histories $\Hist$,
    \item $\Obs : \Hist \to \mathcal{P}(O)$ maps each history to a
          nonempty set of admissible \emph{outcomes},
    \item $\Ord$ is a partial order on outcomes, where $o_1 \Ord o_2$
          means $o_2$ \emph{refines} $o_1$ without contradicting it.
  \end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\noindent
The outcome order $\Ord$ is part of the specification---it is not
derived from execution structure but declared as part of the semantic
contract.
It encodes which transitions between outcomes merely add detail (upward
in $\Ord$, compatible with prior observations) and which exclude
alternatives (incomparable or downward, contradicting prior
observations).
Different specifications over the same histories may use different
outcome orders.

\begin{remark}[Choosing the outcome order]
  The outcome order $\Ord$ is a modeling choice, but in practice it
  follows from the structure of the output.
  Typical choices:
  if the output is an ordered stream, $\Ord$ is prefix containment;
  if the output is an unordered collection, $\Ord$ is set or multiset
  containment;
  if the output is a value in a domain with a natural partial order
  (e.g., a lattice), $\Ord$ is that order.
  A too-coarse order (e.g., a top element refining everything) makes
  all specifications trivially monotone; a too-fine order (recording
  irrelevant implementation details) introduces spurious
  non-monotonicity.
  The outcome order is part of the specification's semantic
  contract---it is determined by what the specification \emph{means},
  not chosen freely by the analyst.
  Changing the order changes the specification.
  The applications in Section~\ref{sec:applications} illustrate natural
  choices: prefix extension for transaction logs, set inclusion for
  committed facts, and lattice order for CRDT states.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Outcomes and order for the register]
  \label{ex:register-outcomes}
  In the register example, outcomes are sequences of completed
  operations annotated with return values---e.g.,
  $\langle w(1), r \mapsto 1 \rangle$ records that a write of $1$
  completed, then a read returned $1$.
  The outcome order $\Ord$ is prefix extension: $o_1 \Ord o_2$ iff
  $o_1$ is a prefix of $o_2$.
  A longer sequence refines a shorter one by recording additional
  completed operations.

  At history $H_1$ (Example~\ref{ex:h1-formal}), neither the write nor
  the read has completed, so the empty sequence $\langle\rangle$ is
  admissible.
  If the read completes returning $0$, the outcome
  $\langle r \mapsto 0 \rangle$ is admissible under both
  $\Spec_{\mathit{ec}}$ and $\Spec_{\mathit{lin}}$.
  Under $\Spec_{\mathit{lin}}$, this outcome becomes
  future-inconsistent at $H_2$: no outcome in
  $\Obs_{\mathit{lin}}(H_2)$ extends it, because every linearization of
  $H_2$ requires the read to return $1$.
\end{example}

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Implementations and Coordination-Freedom}
\label{sec:implementations}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

A specification is passive: it declares which outcomes are admissible but
says nothing about which histories actually arise or which outcomes are
actually exposed.
An \emph{implementation} is active: it determines which histories are
realized (by choosing how to schedule, route, and deliver messages) and
which outcomes it exposes at each history.

The central question is: must an implementation ever \emph{suppress}
histories that the specification would permit?
If so, it is coordinating---preventing causally admissible futures in
order to avoid exposing outcomes that would later be contradicted.
If not, it is coordination-free.

\begin{definition}[Input projection]
  For a history $H = (E, \rightarrow)$, the \emph{input projection}
  $\In(H)$ restricts $H$ to its input events:
  $\In(H) = (E \cap E_{\mathit{in}},\;
  \rightarrow \cap (E_{\mathit{in}} \times E_{\mathit{in}}))$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Admissible histories]
  For an input history $H_{\mathit{in}}$ (consisting only of input
  events), the \emph{admissible histories} are all well-formed histories
  whose input projection extends $H_{\mathit{in}}$:
  \[
    \mathcal{A}(H_{\mathit{in}}) \triangleq
    \{H \in \Hist \mid H_{\mathit{in}} \hext \In(H)\}.
  \]
  These are the histories that \emph{could} arise under asynchrony and
  causality alone, given the input prefix $H_{\mathit{in}}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Implementation]
  \label{def:implementation}
  An implementation $I$ specifies, for each input history
  $H_{\mathit{in}}$:
  \begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item a set of \emph{realizable histories}
          $\mathcal{R}_I(H_{\mathit{in}}) \subseteq
          \mathcal{A}(H_{\mathit{in}})$, closed under prefixes
          (if $H \in \mathcal{R}_I$ and $H' \hext H$, then
          $H' \in \mathcal{R}_I$),
    \item an outcome-exposure map
          $\Expose_I : \Hist \to \mathcal{P}(O)$ with
          $\Expose_I(H) \subseteq \Obs(H)$ for all
          $H \in \mathcal{R}_I(H_{\mathit{in}})$.
  \end{itemize}
  Both $\mathcal{R}_I$ and $\Expose_I$ are required to be
  \emph{computable}: there exists an effective procedure that, given
  $H_{\mathit{in}}$ and $H$, decides whether
  $H \in \mathcal{R}_I(H_{\mathit{in}})$ and enumerates
  $\Expose_I(H)$.
\end{definition}

\noindent
Downward-closure ensures that an implementation cannot ``hide''
intermediate states: every consistent cut of a realizable history
(every downward-closed sub-history) is itself realizable.
This models the physical constraint that a distributed execution passes
through all consistent intermediate states.
The computability requirement excludes pathological implementations that
could not be realized by any effective procedure.
Correctness ($\Expose_I(H) \subseteq \Obs(H)$) is required only at
realizable histories---an implementation is not penalized for behavior at
histories it never produces.

A coordination-free implementation realizes \emph{all} admissible
histories---it never suppresses a causally admissible future:

\begin{definition}[Coordination-free implementation]
  \label{def:coordfree}
  An implementation $I$ is \emph{coordination-free} if for every input
  history $H_{\mathit{in}}$ and every
  $H \in \mathcal{R}_I(H_{\mathit{in}})$:
  \[
    \Poss^I_{H_{\mathit{in}}}(H) = \Poss_{H_{\mathit{in}}}(H),
  \]
  where
  $\Poss_{H_{\mathit{in}}}(H) \triangleq
  \bigcup_{\substack{H' \in \mathcal{A}(H_{\mathit{in}})\\
  H \hext H'}} \Obs(H')$
  collects all outcomes possible under the specification along admissible
  extensions, and
  $\Poss^I_{H_{\mathit{in}}}(H) \triangleq
  \bigcup_{\substack{H' \in \mathcal{R}_I(H_{\mathit{in}})\\
  H \hext H'}} \Expose_I(H')$
  collects those the implementation can still reach.
\end{definition}

\noindent
The condition $\Poss^I = \Poss$ says: every outcome that the
specification permits along some admissible future remains reachable
under the implementation.
The implementation may influence \emph{which} outcome is eventually
exposed (via retries, routing, or internal messages), but it cannot
\emph{prevent} any outcome that remains specification-permitted.

When this condition fails---when
$\Poss^I_{H_{\mathit{in}}}(H) \subsetneq
\Poss_{H_{\mathit{in}}}(H)$---the implementation has ruled out some
admissible future.
That ruling-out is coordination: the implementation suppresses a
causally admissible extension to avoid exposing an outcome that would
later be contradicted.

\begin{example}[Coordination in the register]
  Under $\Spec_{\mathit{lin}}$, suppose an implementation exposes
  $\langle r \mapsto 0 \rangle$ at $H_1$.
  The extension $H_2$ (where $w \rightarrow r$) admits no outcome
  compatible with this choice.
  To remain correct, the implementation must either:
  (a) avoid exposing $\langle r \mapsto 0 \rangle$ at $H_1$
  (suppressing a permitted outcome), or
  (b) avoid realizing $H_2$ (suppressing an admissible future).
  Either way, $\Poss^I \subsetneq \Poss$: coordination is unavoidable.
\end{example}

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Complete CALM}
\label{sec:complete-calm}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

We can now state the criterion precisely.
The question is: when does a specification force every correct
implementation to suppress admissible futures?
The answer is a single semantic property of the specification.

\begin{definition}[Monotonicity of a specification]
  \label{def:monotone}
  A specification $\Spec = (E, \Obs, \Ord)$ is \emph{monotone} if
  for all $H_1, H_2 \in \Hist$ with $H_1 \hext H_2$ and all
  $o \in \Obs(H_1)$, there exists $o' \in \Obs(H_2)$ with $o \Ord o'$.
\end{definition}

\noindent
This generalizes CALM's notion of monotonicity from a specific mapping
(input facts $\to$ output facts, ordered by set inclusion) to an
arbitrary one (histories $\to$ outcome sets, ordered by any
user-defined refinement $\Ord$).
The definition is the same---outputs refine as inputs grow---but the
domain, range, and order are all generalized.

Monotonicity is a no-regret condition on observable behavior:
once an outcome is admitted at a history, every future of that history
admits a compatible refinement.
No outcome, once permitted, can be ``stranded'' by a future extension.

A failure of monotonicity is witnessed by a
\emph{future-inconsistent outcome}: an outcome $o \in \Obs(H_1)$ such
that some future $H_2$ of $H_1$ admits no $o' \in \Obs(H_2)$ with
$o \Ord o'$.
The register witness from Section~\ref{sec:example} is exactly such a
failure: $\langle r \mapsto 0 \rangle$ is admitted at $H_1$ but
stranded at $H_2$.

\begin{theorem}[Complete CALM]
  \label{thm:complete-calm}
  A specification $\Spec = (E, \Obs, \Ord)$ admits a coordination-free
  implementation if and only if it is monotone.
\end{theorem}

\begin{example}[Applying Complete CALM to the running example]
  $\Spec_{\mathit{ec}}$ is monotone: every admitted read value
  remains compatible with all extensions (a stale read is never
  contradicted by future deliveries---it simply becomes less current).
  By Complete CALM, eventual consistency admits a coordination-free
  implementation.

  $\Spec_{\mathit{lin}}$ is not monotone (the witness from
  Section~\ref{sec:example}).
  By Complete CALM, every correct implementation of linearizability must
  coordinate---it must suppress some admissible future to avoid
  stranding an exposed outcome.
\end{example}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
  \emph{Sufficiency.}
  If $\Spec$ is monotone, set
  $\mathcal{R}_I(H_{\mathit{in}}) = \mathcal{A}(H_{\mathit{in}})$
  (realize all admissible histories) and set
  $\Expose_I(H) = \Obs(H)$ (the implementation may expose any
  outcome the specification permits at $H$).
  Correctness holds immediately.
  For possibility preservation: since $\mathcal{R}_I = \mathcal{A}$,
  every admissible extension is realized.
  Since $\Expose_I(H) = \Obs(H)$, every specification-permitted
  outcome at every realized history is exposable.
  Hence $\Poss^I_{H_{\mathit{in}}}(H) = \Poss_{H_{\mathit{in}}}(H)$
  and the implementation is coordination-free.
  (Monotonicity ensures this implementation is \emph{correct}:
  exposing $o \in \Obs(H)$ is safe because every future $H'$ admits
  some $o' \in \Obs(H')$ with $o \Ord o'$---the exposed outcome is
  never contradicted.)

  \emph{Necessity.}
  If $\Spec$ is not monotone, there exist $H_1 \hext H_2$ and
  $o_1 \in \Obs(H_1)$ such that no $o' \in \Obs(H_2)$ satisfies
  $o_1 \Ord o'$.
  Both $H_1$ and $H_2$ are admissible (they share the same input
  projection).
  Any coordination-free implementation must realize both.
  But if it exposes $o_1$ at $H_1$, the extension $H_2$ admits no
  compatible outcome---correctness is violated.
  To remain correct, the implementation must either suppress $o_1$
  (ruling out a specification-permitted outcome at $H_1$) or suppress
  $H_2$ (ruling out an admissible future).
  Either way, $\Poss^I \subsetneq \Poss$, contradicting
  coordination-freedom.
  Hence no coordination-free implementation exists.

  The full proof, including a tightness argument showing that
  monotonicity is the weakest sufficient condition, appears in
  Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}.
\end{proof}

\noindent
The characterization is tight: monotonicity is the weakest
semantic property that guarantees well-coordination.
Any specification admitting even one future-inconsistent outcome
requires coordination (by the necessity direction), so no strictly
weaker condition suffices.
Conversely, monotonicity is the strongest condition implied by
well-coordination---every well-coordinated specification is
monotone.
The three components of a specification (event universe, observability
function, outcome order) are each unconstrained, and the definition of
coordination-freedom (possibility preservation) is the canonical
semantic notion for asynchronous message-passing.
Within this setting, no further generalization of the characterization
is possible.

Because Complete CALM operates on semantic specifications rather than
program syntax, checking monotonicity of an arbitrary specification is
not a decidable property in general (analogous to Rice's theorem for
semantic properties of programs).
The criterion is an analytic tool for proving coordination bounds---as
demonstrated by the applications in Section~\ref{sec:applications}---not
a compiler-checkable syntactic condition.
For specifications presented in restricted formalisms (e.g., Datalog
programs, CRDT definitions), decidability depends on the formalism.

\paragraph{CAP and authority.}
Complete CALM diagnoses that linearizability requires coordination---an
ordering authority must exist.
This is a semantic fact about the specification.
Everything else in the CAP discourse concerns the \emph{failure modes of
authority mechanisms}, which are operational, not semantic.
A single-leader authority is available to all nodes that can reach it but
may crash; a quorum-based authority tolerates individual crashes but may
become unavailable if no partition contains a majority.
Both are engineering tradeoffs in how authority is implemented, not
consequences of the specification's semantic structure.
Ameloot et al.~\cite{ameloot2013relational} pointed in this direction by
showing that coordination-freedom in the transducer model depends on what
system relations nodes can access---a precursor to separating the semantic
need from the mechanism.
Complete CALM makes the separation explicit: it diagnoses \emph{whether}
authority is needed (a property of the specification); CAP-style results
characterize when particular authority mechanisms become unavailable
(a property of the failure model and the mechanism design).


% ================================================================
\section{Strict Generalization of CALM}
\label{sec:beyond-calm}
% ================================================================

Complete CALM operates on observable output semantics; the original CALM
operates on program syntax.
When do these diverge?
We show that CALM is a direct instance of Complete CALM under a natural
modeling choice, and then exhibit a strict separation: settings where
Complete CALM correctly identifies coordination-free behavior that CALM
cannot recognize.

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{CALM as an Instance}
\label{sec:calm-instance}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

Recall the CALM setting: nodes in a relational transducer
network~\cite{ameloot2013relational} hold local relations, receive
input facts over time, exchange messages, and derive output facts.
A program is \emph{monotone} if its output grows monotonically (under set
inclusion) as input facts arrive.

This is an instance of Complete CALM under the following
modeling choice:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \emph{Histories} are executions of the transducer network: input-fact
        arrivals are external input events; rule firings and message exchanges
        are internal events.
  \item \emph{Outcomes} are output-fact sets, ordered by set inclusion:
        $o_1 \Ord o_2$ iff $o_1 \subseteq o_2$.
  \item \emph{Observability:} $\Obs(H) = \{\mathit{LFP}(H)\}$, the least
        fixpoint of the program over the input facts present in $H$.
\end{itemize}
Under this specification, monotonicity coincides exactly with
program monotonicity: extending a history can only add input facts, and a
monotone program's least fixpoint can only grow under set inclusion.
Conversely, a non-monotone program admits inputs $I_1 \subseteq I_2$ with
$\mathit{LFP}(I_1) \not\subseteq \mathit{LFP}(I_2)$, yielding a
future-inconsistent outcome.
Complete CALM therefore recovers CALM immediately.

The key observation is that CALM \emph{equates program outputs with
observable commitments}.
This is a modeling choice, not a necessity.
Complete CALM operates on observable output semantics---what
an implementation exposes---rather than on program text.
When these diverge, the criterion remains correct while CALM does not.

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The Separation: Coordination Discharges Non-Monotonicity}
\label{sec:separation}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

The central insight is simple: coordination is a \emph{means}, not an
\emph{end}.
A system may use coordination internally to resolve a non-monotone
specification, and then expose the resolved output to downstream
consumers.
From the consumers' perspective, the output stream is monotone---it only
grows---even though the internal logic that produced it was not.
CALM, applied to the combined system, sees non-monotone operators and
concludes that coordination is required.
Complete CALM, applied to the observable output, correctly
identifies it as coordination-free.

We make this precise.
A coordination mechanism restricts which histories can arise---it
suppresses some admissible futures to enforce correctness.
The \emph{resolved specification} is what remains after this restriction:

\begin{definition}[Resolved specification]
  Let $\Spec = (E, \Obs, \Ord)$ be a specification and let $I$ be a
  coordination mechanism (an implementation that restricts admissible
  histories to enforce correctness).
  The \emph{resolved specification} $\Spec|_I$ is the specification
  whose histories are those realized by $I$ and whose observability
  function is $\Obs$ restricted to those histories.
  Intuitively, $\Spec|_I$ describes the world as seen by a downstream
  consumer who observes only the outputs that $I$ produces.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Separation from CALM]
  \label{thm:separation}
  There exist specifications $\Spec$ such that:
  \begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item $\Spec$ is not monotone (coordination is required),
    \item there exists a correct coordination mechanism $I$ such that
          the resolved specification $\Spec|_I$ \emph{is} monotone,
    \item CALM, applied syntactically to any program implementing $I$,
          classifies it as non-monotone (requiring coordination).
  \end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\noindent
In other words: Complete CALM can verify that coordination
\emph{correctly discharges} a non-monotone specification, producing
coordination-free downstream behavior.
CALM cannot make this distinction---it sees only program syntax.

\begin{proof}
  We exhibit a concrete witness.
  Consider a key-value store replicated at two nodes, with a
  Paxos-based total-order broadcast ensuring all nodes process updates
  in the same sequence.
  The internal specification is linearizability (non-monotone, as shown
  in Section~\ref{sec:example}).
  Paxos provides the coordination: it restricts admissible histories to
  those in which all updates are totally ordered.

  The \emph{exposed output} is the prefix of the ordered update log that
  each node has processed.
  Define outcomes as log prefixes, ordered by prefix extension:
  $o_1 \Ord o_2$ iff $o_1$ is a prefix of $o_2$.
  Under this observability:
  \begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item At history $H$, $\Obs(H)$ is the set of prefixes of the
          Paxos-ordered log consistent with $H$.
    \item For any future $H' \sqsupseteq_h H$: the log can only grow.
          Any prefix $o \in \Obs(H)$ remains a prefix of every
          $o' \in \Obs(H')$.
          Hence $o \Ord o'$.
  \end{itemize}
  The resolved specification is monotone.
  By Complete CALM, the output stream admits
  coordination-free consumption: downstream readers need no additional
  coordination to observe a consistent, growing log.

  Now consider CALM's analysis.
  Any program implementing Paxos uses non-monotone operations:
  leader election requires negation (``no higher ballot has been
  seen''), and log commitment requires universal quantification over a
  quorum (``all members of the quorum have acknowledged'').
  CALM, applied to the combined program text, identifies these
  non-monotone operators and classifies the system as requiring
  coordination.
  This is correct for the \emph{internal} specification
  (linearizability does require coordination), but incorrect for the
  \emph{output} specification (the exposed log prefix is
  coordination-free to consume).
  Complete CALM distinguishes these; CALM conflates them.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Stratified Datalog: the canonical separation]
  \label{ex:stratified-separation}
  The separation is not limited to distributed systems---it arises in
  CALM's own native setting.
  Consider a stratified Datalog program with two strata:
  \begin{align*}
    \mathit{reachable}(X,Y) &\leftarrow \mathit{edge}(X,Y). \\
    \mathit{reachable}(X,Z) &\leftarrow \mathit{reachable}(X,Y),\,
                               \mathit{edge}(Y,Z). \\[3pt]
    \mathit{unreachable}(X,Y) &\leftarrow \mathit{node}(X),\,
                                 \mathit{node}(Y),\,
                                 \neg\,\mathit{reachable}(X,Y).
  \end{align*}
  CALM classifies this program as non-monotone: the negation in stratum~2
  means that adding an edge fact can cause an $\mathit{unreachable}$ tuple
  to be retracted.
  Coordination is required to evaluate it---specifically, stratum~1 must
  be \emph{sealed} (a commitment that no further edge facts will arrive)
  before stratum~2 can execute.
  Without this guarantee, a negated literal $\neg\,\mathit{reachable}(X,Y)$
  might be evaluated prematurely and later invalidated.

  But consider the system that \emph{performs} this coordination and then
  exposes the result.
  After sealing, the output of stratum~2 is fixed: no further edge facts
  will arrive, so no $\mathit{unreachable}$ tuple will ever be retracted.
  The exposed output---the final $\mathit{unreachable}$ relation---is
  monotone under set inclusion (it can only grow as the sealed computation
  completes).
  Complete CALM correctly identifies this resolved output as
  monotone.

  CALM, applied to the program text, sees $\neg\,\mathit{reachable}$ and
  reports ``non-monotone, requires coordination.''
  It cannot distinguish between the program \emph{before} sealing
  (genuinely non-monotone, coordination needed) and the program
  \emph{after} sealing (resolved, coordination-free).
  Complete CALM makes exactly this distinction.
\end{example}

\noindent
The pattern is general.
Hellerstein~\cite{hellerstein2026complexity} defines the
\emph{determination depth} of a specification as the minimum number of
sequential coordination layers needed to resolve it.
A specification with determination depth $k$ requires $k$ layers of
coordination; after those layers are discharged, the resolved output is
monotone and all downstream computation is coordination-free.
CALM operates at the boundary $k = 0$ vs.\ $k \geq 1$ but cannot see
past it: it treats a depth-$k$ specification identically whether the
coordination has been provided or not.
Complete CALM operates on the output semantics and
correctly identifies the post-coordination regime as coordination-free.

\paragraph{The architectural pattern.}
The separation is not academic---it captures a ubiquitous systems design.
A coordination layer (Paxos, Raft, ZAB) resolves non-monotonicity
internally, producing an append-only ordered log.
A consumption layer (log tailing, CDC, materialized views, stream
processors) reads from this log without any additional coordination.
Complete CALM provides the formal basis for this separation: the log's
prefix-monotone output semantics are monotone, certifying that
downstream consumers need not coordinate, regardless of the non-monotone
logic that produced the log.

The next subsection shows this is not merely an observation about one
architecture---it is a universal construction.

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{One Round of Coordination Universally Suffices}
\label{sec:universal-construction}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

The separation theorem exhibits specifications where coordination
resolves non-monotonicity and produces monotone output.
We now show this is always possible: for \emph{any} specification, a
single round of distributed coordination---establishing
membership---suffices to reduce all remaining computation to
coordination-free local evaluation.

The key insight is that membership knowledge converts universal
quantification into existential quantification.
A non-monotone operation like ``all participants have contributed''
requires knowing who the participants \emph{are} before it can be
evaluated.
Once membership is established, the check becomes monotone: the set of
received contributions only grows, and once it equals the known
membership, the condition fires.
This is the same mechanism underlying the Immerman--Vardi
theorem~\cite{immerman1986relational}: bounded quantification over a
known domain is expressible in fixpoint logic.

\begin{theorem}[Universal sufficiency of membership]
  \label{thm:universal}
  For any specification $\Spec = (E, \Obs, \Ord)$, there exists a
  coordination mechanism $I_{\mathit{mem}}$ that establishes membership
  (makes the participant set common knowledge) such that the resolved
  specification $\Spec|_{I_{\mathit{mem}}}$ is monotone.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
  \emph{Construction.}
  Let $I_{\mathit{mem}}$ establish membership: a designated seed
  authority publishes the set of participants $\mathsf{All}$.
  Once membership is known, the mechanism places all input events into
  a total order consistent with causality and delivers this order
  identically to every replica.
  This is an \emph{online} process: events are ordered and delivered
  incrementally as they arrive, not batched.
  Any protocol that produces a consistent total extension of the causal
  partial order suffices (e.g., total-order broadcast, replicated state
  machines, or any consensus-based sequencer).
  Different runs may produce different total orders---the choice among
  valid extensions is nondeterministic---but within a run, all replicas
  observe the same sequence.

  Each replica evaluates the specification deterministically over the
  growing prefix of ordered events.
  Define the resolved specification's outcomes as these prefix-indexed
  evaluation results, ordered by prefix extension:
  $o_1 \Ord o_2$ iff $o_1$ corresponds to a prefix of the sequence
  that $o_2$ corresponds to.

  \emph{Monotonicity.}
  The ordered sequence only grows (events are appended, never
  reordered or retracted).
  Hence the evaluation result at any prefix refines monotonically:
  for $H_1 \hext H_2$, the prefix at $H_2$ extends the prefix at
  $H_1$, so $o_1 \Ord o_2$.
  The resolved specification is monotone.

  \emph{Why membership is the only distributed coordination needed.}
  Once membership is established, the ordering mechanism operates by
  monotone accumulation: each replica receives an ever-growing prefix
  of the agreed sequence.
  No replica needs to suppress any admissible future---it simply
  processes events as they arrive in the agreed order.
  By Complete CALM, this consumption phase is coordination-free.
\end{proof}

\noindent
The construction shows that membership is the \emph{universal}
coordination primitive: once participants are known, all remaining
non-monotonicity can be resolved by monotone local computation
(accumulate inputs from known participants, then evaluate
deterministically).
No further distributed coordination---no suppression of admissible
futures---is needed.

\paragraph{Comparison with Ameloot et al.}
Ameloot et al.~\cite{ameloot2013relational} prove a related result in
the transducer model: if nodes have access to the system relations
$\mathsf{All}$ (the set of all node identifiers) and $\mathsf{Id}$
(the local identifier)---the \emph{non-oblivious} setting---then
monotone queries can be evaluated coordination-free.
Our construction improves on this in two ways.

First, it requires no syntactic recognition of special relations.
Ameloot's proof depends on the program having access to $\mathsf{All}$
and $\mathsf{Id}$ and on the analysis recognizing these as system
relations with specific semantics.
Our construction is purely semantic: it operates on the specification
$\Spec$ without inspecting any program text.
Membership is a generic coordination mechanism, not a model-specific
assumption about available relations.

Second, it is robust to membership change.
Ameloot's non-obliviousness assumes a fixed, globally known set of
nodes---the $\mathsf{All}$ relation is static.
If membership changes (nodes join or depart), the preconditions of the
theorem fail.
Our construction accommodates membership change because the authority
that established membership can extend it: the current authority uses
its decision-making power to appoint the next authority or to admit new
members.
This appointment is itself a monotone extension of the authority
chain---each membership-change event extends the history without
retracting prior membership decisions.
The chain of authorization is constructed incrementally from an original
seed authority (typically defined offline in a configuration file at
system setup).
Downstream coordination-freedom is a property of the \emph{output
semantics} (monotone accumulation over known membership), not of any
static membership assumption.

\paragraph{Architectural consequence.}
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal} formally justifies a common systems
pattern: establish membership once (via a configuration service,
bootstrap protocol, or seed authority), then process all subsequent
computation coordination-free.
Log-based architectures (Kafka~\cite{kreps2011kafka}, event sourcing,
change data capture) are one realization: the log's sequencer is the
membership authority, and downstream consumers accumulate log entries
monotonically.
But the theorem is more general---any architecture that establishes
membership and then accumulates inputs monotonically achieves
coordination-freedom downstream, regardless of the specification's
non-monotone structure.

\begin{remark}[Distributed vs.\ local coordination]
  \label{rem:local-depth}
  The universal construction resolves all \emph{distributed}
  coordination---the need for cross-node communication to suppress
  admissible futures---in one round (membership).
  However, local evaluation may still require sequential commitment
  that does not involve distributed coordination.

  Consider a multi-stratum Datalog program evaluated across $n$ nodes.
  Each stratum must be \emph{sealed} before the next can begin: a node
  must know that all derivations in stratum $k$ are complete before
  evaluating negation in stratum $k{+}1$.
  This sealing requires waiting for end-of-data signals from all
  participants---but that waiting is \emph{monotone}: the set of
  received signals only grows, and once all have arrived, the seal
  commits locally.
  No distributed coordination is needed beyond knowing who the
  participants are (membership).

  The distributed coordination depth is therefore just one round:
  establishing membership.
  After that, each stratum seal is a local commitment triggered by
  monotone accumulation of signals from known participants.
  The $k$ strata require $k$ local sequential commitments, but zero
  additional rounds of distributed coordination.
  Hellerstein~\cite{hellerstein2026complexity} characterizes this full
  sequential cost (including local commitments) as \emph{determination
  depth}; Complete CALM addresses only the distributed boundary.
\end{remark}

\section{Applications to Data Management}
\label{sec:applications}
% ================================================================

We instantiate Complete CALM on three families of specifications central
to data management.
In each case, the same semantic test---monotonicity---recovers a
known coordination boundary and provides a uniform explanation for why it
arises.

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Transactional Isolation Levels}
\label{sec:isolation}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

Bailis et al.~\cite{bailis2013hat} identify a sharp boundary between
isolation levels achievable with high availability (HATs) and those
requiring coordination.
We recover this boundary as the monotonicity boundary.

\paragraph{Spec sketch.}
Histories include transaction invocations, read/write events, and commit
decisions.
Outcomes are sets of atomic facts
$\{\mathsf{commit}(T), \mathsf{read}(T, x, v), \ldots\}$
recording committed transactions and their observed values.
The outcome order is set inclusion: $o_1 \Ord o_2$ iff $o_1 \subseteq o_2$.
An isolation level $L$ induces $\Spec_L = (E, \Obs_L, \Ord)$ where
$\Obs_L(H)$ contains the outcome fact-sets consistent with $L$'s
correctness constraints.

\begin{proposition}[HAT levels are monotone]
  \label{prop:hat-monotone}
  Under read uncommitted, read committed, and session guarantees, the
  constraints are prefix-stable: if $o \in \Obs_L(H)$ and $H \hext H'$,
  there exists $o' \in \Obs_L(H')$ with $o \subseteq o'$.
  Extending a history may add committed transactions but cannot
  invalidate previously exposed commit or read facts.
  Hence $\Spec_L$ is monotone, and by Complete CALM these levels
  admit coordination-free implementations.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Serializability is not monotone]
  \label{prop:ser-nonmonotone}
  Under serializability, there exist $H_1 \hext H_2$ and
  $o_1 \in \Obs_{\mathit{ser}}(H_1)$ with no extension in
  $\Obs_{\mathit{ser}}(H_2)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Witness]
  Let $x = y = 0$ initially.
  Transaction $T_1$ reads $x$, writes $y := 1$;
  $T_2$ reads $y$, writes $x := 1$.
  At $H_1$, $T_1$ commits after reading $x = 0$:
  $o_1 = \{\mathsf{commit}(T_1), \mathsf{read}(T_1, x, 0)\}
  \in \Obs_{\mathit{ser}}(H_1)$.
  Extend to $H_2$ where $T_2$ also commits reading $y = 0$.
  Any $o_2 \supseteq o_1$ in $\Obs_{\mathit{ser}}(H_2)$ must include
  both reads, but $\mathsf{read}(T_1, x, 0)$ and
  $\mathsf{read}(T_2, y, 0)$ together induce a cyclic serialization
  order.
  No such $o_2$ exists.
\end{proof}

\noindent
The same argument applies to snapshot isolation (write-skew creates the
cycle).
By Complete CALM, serializability and snapshot isolation intrinsically
require coordination.
This recovers the HAT/non-HAT boundary of~\cite{bailis2013hat} as a
single semantic test.

The structural explanation is general: specifications that commit only to
partial-order structure (causal consistency, read committed) are
monotone---a downward-closed cut of happens-before remains valid
under extension.
Specifications that commit to total-order structure across concurrency
(serializability, snapshot isolation) are not---a linearization commits
to adjacency facts that a concurrent extension can invalidate.
The HAT/non-HAT boundary is precisely the partial-order/total-order
boundary.

\paragraph{Why CALM cannot address this directly.}
Transactional isolation is not naturally expressed as a Datalog program.
One could encode a transaction scheduler in Datalog, but the encoding is
not unique: a scheduler that checks serializability via cycle detection
uses negation (non-monotone), while one that enforces it via two-phase
locking uses only monotone lock acquisition---yet both implement the same
specification.
CALM's diagnosis would depend on which encoding is chosen, not on the
isolation level itself.
Complete CALM sidesteps this entirely: it tests the specification
directly, without any program encoding.

\paragraph{Connection to CAP.}
The preceding analysis is about concurrency: serializability requires
coordination whether the database is distributed or not.
CAP adds the distributional consequence: when the system \emph{is}
distributed, the required coordination may become unavailable under
partitions.
Specifically, the non-monotonicity of serializability means an ordering
authority is required.
Under partitions, that authority may become unavailable (depending on its
implementation mechanism), forcing a choice between blocking and weakening
the isolation guarantee.
Complete CALM identifies the \emph{source} of this dilemma---non-monotonicity
of the specification---while CAP describes its \emph{operational
manifestation} under particular failure models.

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Invariant Confluence}
\label{sec:i-confluence}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

Bailis et al.~\cite{bailis2014coordination} show that an application
invariant can be preserved without coordination iff it is
\emph{$I$-confluent}: merging any two invariant-preserving states yields
an invariant-preserving state.
We recover this as an instance of Complete CALM.

\paragraph{Spec sketch.}
Consider a replicated database where each node applies transactions
locally and periodically merges with other replicas.
Outcomes are sets of database states reachable under causally admissible
extensions, ordered by set inclusion (refinement = ruling out states).
$\Obs_I(H)$ contains the invariant-preserving subsets of reachable
states at $H$.
For example, if the invariant is ``account balances are non-negative,''
then $\Obs_I(H)$ contains all subsets of reachable states in which every
account has non-negative balance.

\begin{proposition}[$I$-confluence $\Leftrightarrow$ monotonicity]
  \label{prop:iconfl}
  $\Spec_I$ is monotone iff $I$ is $I$-confluent.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
  If $I$ is $I$-confluent: extending a history can only enlarge the
  reachable-state set, and merging invariant-preserving states preserves
  the invariant.
  Hence every outcome at a prefix has a refinement at every extension.

  If $I$ is not $I$-confluent: there exist reachable states $s_1, s_2$
  both satisfying $I$ whose merge violates $I$.
  The singleton $\{s_1\}$ is an outcome at some prefix that admits no
  invariant-preserving extension including the merge---a
  future-inconsistent outcome.
\end{proof}

\noindent
By Complete CALM, $I$-confluent invariants admit coordination-free
enforcement; non-$I$-confluent invariants require coordination.
The criterion provides the \emph{why}: non-$I$-confluence is exactly
non-monotonicity of observable state sets under causal extension.

\paragraph{Why CALM is fragile here.}
To apply CALM to invariant enforcement, one must encode the invariant
check as a Datalog program.
A foreign-key constraint (non-monotone: deletion of a referenced tuple
violates it) and a uniqueness constraint (non-monotone: insertion of a
duplicate violates it) would both be classified as requires
coordination'' by CALM.
But $I$-confluence distinguishes them: foreign keys are not
$I$-confluent (merging two valid states can violate the constraint),
while uniqueness under certain merge strategies is.
Complete CALM recovers this distinction directly from the specification,
without encoding invariants as Datalog rules.

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{CRDTs and Replicated Objects}
\label{sec:crdts}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

Conflict-free replicated data types~\cite{shapiro2011crdt} achieve
coordination-free replication by restricting updates to inflationary
operations on a join-semilattice.
Complete CALM explains why this works.

\paragraph{Spec sketch.}
A state-based CRDT has state space $(S, \sqcup)$ forming a
join-semilattice.
Updates are inflationary: $\mathit{update}(s) \sqsupseteq s$.
Outcomes are states, ordered by the lattice order:
$o_1 \Ord o_2$ iff $o_1 \sqsubseteq o_2$.
$\Obs(H)$ contains the states reachable by applying updates and merges
consistent with $H$.

\begin{proposition}[CRDTs are monotone]
  \label{prop:crdt-monotone}
  Any specification whose updates are inflationary in a
  join-semilattice and whose outcome order is the lattice order is
  monotone.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  Let $o \in \Obs(H)$ and $H \hext H'$.
  The extension $H'$ may add updates and merges.
  Since updates are inflationary ($\mathit{update}(s) \sqsupseteq s$)
  and merge is join ($s_1 \sqcup s_2 \sqsupseteq s_1$), every state
  reachable at $H'$ is at least as large (in the lattice order) as
  some state reachable at $H$.
  In particular, there exists $o' \in \Obs(H')$ with
  $o \sqsubseteq o'$, i.e., $o \Ord o'$.
\end{proof}

\noindent
CRDTs are monotone \emph{by construction}: the lattice structure
guarantees that observable state can only grow.
Complete CALM certifies that this structural choice is both sufficient
and, in a precise sense, necessary: any coordination-free replicated
object must have monotone observable semantics, and the
semilattice discipline is one way to ensure this.

The converse is instructive.
A replicated counter with a \emph{reset} operation (set to zero) is not
inflationary: after incrementing to $5$, a reset produces a state
incomparable to $5$ under any natural order.
If the outcome order treats $0$ and $5$ as contradictory, the
specification is not monotone and coordination is required to
implement reset consistently.
This matches the known result that reset-capable CRDTs require
additional protocol machinery~\cite{shapiro2011crdt}.

\paragraph{CALM and CRDTs.}
CRDTs were designed independently of CALM, using lattice theory rather
than Datalog monotonicity.
CALM can analyze a CRDT only if its merge and update logic is first
encoded as a Datalog program---an unnatural exercise that obscures the
lattice structure.
Complete CALM applies directly to the CRDT's specification (states
ordered by the lattice order), recognizing monotonicity as a
consequence of the semilattice structure without any encoding step.

\medskip
\noindent
Across all three application families, the same phenomenon recurs:
coordination is required exactly when observable outcomes commit to
structure that a future extension can invalidate.
For isolation levels, the fragile structure is a global serialization
order; for invariant confluence, it is a reachable-state set closed
under merge; for CRDTs, it is the lattice order itself.
Monotonicity is the uniform diagnostic that identifies this
boundary in each case.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ================================================================

\paragraph{CALM and relational transducers.}
The CALM theorem, conjectured by Hellerstein~\cite{hellerstein2010declarative}
and proved by Ameloot et al.~\cite{ameloot2013relational}, establishes that
monotone queries admit coordination-free distributed evaluation in the
relational transducer model.
Complete CALM subsumes this: relational transducers with
set-inclusion order on output facts are one instantiation of our framework
(Section~\ref{sec:calm-instance}).
The key advance is that our criterion operates on \emph{observable output
semantics} rather than program syntax, enabling verification that
coordination correctly discharges a non-monotone specification---a
capability absent from the transducer-based analysis.

\paragraph{Baccaert and Ketsman: coordination spectrum.}
Baccaert and Ketsman~\cite{baccaert2023dcbq,baccaert2026spectrum} generalize
CALM by extending the transducer model to
non-deterministic ``behaviors'' and parameterizing system relations,
yielding a coordination spectrum based on shared information.
Their generalization stays \emph{within} the transducer computational model
but varies what information nodes possess.
Ours leaves the transducer model entirely: we characterize coordination
requirements from the specification alone, independent of any computational
model.
The two generalizations are orthogonal:
their spectrum classifies problems by \emph{information requirements};
ours classifies specifications by \emph{commitment structure}.
Crucially, their analysis---like CALM---operates on program text and
cannot recognize that a non-monotone program wrapped with coordination
produces monotone outputs (Theorem~\ref{thm:separation}).

\paragraph{Li and Lee: replicated-object coordination-freedom.}
Li and Lee~\cite{li2025coordinationfree} develop a CALM-style equivalence
in a replicated-object architecture, characterizing coordination-freedom
in terms of replica consistency under growing input sets.
Their model assumes replication and merge semantics as primitives.
Our framework treats replication as one instance rather than an
assumption: the future relation is causal (via happens-before), not
set-theoretic.
Like Baccaert and Ketsman, their analysis is syntactic and does not
capture the separation of Theorem~\ref{thm:separation}.

\paragraph{Determination complexity.}
Hellerstein~\cite{hellerstein2026complexity} introduces \emph{determination
depth}---the minimum sequential layers of irrevocable commitment needed to
resolve a specification---and proves exponential depth--width separations
and a conservation law.
Complete CALM characterizes the depth-$0$ boundary of that hierarchy:
a specification has determination depth $0$ iff it is monotone
iff it admits a coordination-free implementation.

\paragraph{Bailis et al.: HATs and I-confluence.}
Highly Available Transactions~\cite{bailis2013hat} identify isolation levels
achievable without coordination; I-confluence~\cite{bailis2014coordination}
characterizes invariants preservable without coordination.
Both are instances of Complete CALM (Section~\ref{sec:applications}).

\paragraph{CRDTs and eventual consistency.}
Shapiro et al.~\cite{shapiro2011crdt} ensure coordination-free replication
by restricting updates to inflationary operations on join-semilattices.
In our terms, CRDTs inhabit a well-behaved subspace of monotone
specifications (Section~\ref{sec:crdts}).
Burckhardt~\cite{burckhardt2014principles} provides a comprehensive
framework for eventual consistency; our criterion provides the sharp
boundary between specifications that admit it and those that do not.

\paragraph{CAP and partition tolerance.}
Brewer's conjecture~\cite{brewer2000towards} and the Gilbert--Lynch
proof~\cite{gilbert2002cap} show that strong consistency, availability,
and partition tolerance cannot coexist.
Complete CALM reframes this: the semantic content is that non-monotone
specifications require an ordering authority (diagnosed by the criterion);
CAP's operational content is that authority mechanisms have failure modes
under partitions.
The two concerns are cleanly separated in our framework.

\paragraph{Arbitration-free consistency.}
Attiya, Enea, and Rom\'{a}n-Calvo~\cite{attiya2023arbitration} characterize
consistency conditions achievable without arbitration (a form of
coordination).
Their ``arbitration-free'' corresponds to our ``coordination-free'' for
specifications whose outcome order captures the relevant consistency
guarantee.

\paragraph{Type-level monotonicity.}
Milano et al.~\cite{milano2019tour} (Gallifrey) and
Laddad et al.~\cite{laddad2024flo} (Flo) enforce monotonicity through
type systems, requiring programmers to specify the order with respect to
which values evolve.
This design choice influenced our decision to make the outcome order
$\Ord$ a first-class component of specifications: the type-level
discipline ensures monotonicity by construction, just as CRDTs do
at the data-structure level.


% ================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ================================================================

Complete CALM provides a universal semantic criterion for
coordination-freedom: a specification admits a coordination-free
implementation if and only if its observable outcomes are
monotone.
The criterion operates on specifications directly---independent of
programming language, computational model, or protocol structure---and
strictly generalizes the original CALM theorem.

The separation from CALM is not merely theoretical.
It captures a pattern that pervades modern data infrastructure:
coordination layers (consensus protocols, two-phase commit, serializable
schedulers) resolve non-monotonicity internally, producing monotone
output streams that downstream systems consume without coordination.
Complete CALM provides the first formal basis for reasoning about this
separation---verifying that coordination \emph{correctly discharges} a
non-monotone specification, rather than merely detecting that
non-monotonicity exists.

The applications demonstrate that known coordination boundaries---HAT
isolation levels, $I$-confluence, CRDT semilattice
discipline---are instances of a single semantic test.
Monotonicity is the common structural property; its failure is
the universal explanation for why coordination is required.

Hellerstein~\cite{hellerstein2026complexity} develops the quantitative
theory beyond the boundary: \emph{determination depth} measures how many
sequential layers of coordination are needed to resolve a non-monotone
specification, with exponential depth--width separations and a
conservation law showing that computation cannot reduce this cost.
Complete CALM characterizes the depth-$0$ boundary of that hierarchy.
Together, the two results provide a complete picture: Complete CALM
diagnoses \emph{whether} coordination is needed; determination depth
measures \emph{how much}.

\section{Proof of Complete CALM}
\label{app:proofs}

We restate and fully prove Theorem~\ref{thm:complete-calm}.

\begin{theorem}[Complete CALM, restated]
  A specification $\Spec=(E,\Obs,\Ord)$ admits a coordination-free
  implementation if and only if it is monotone.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
  We prove both directions.
  Throughout, coordination-freedom means that for every input history
  $H_{\mathit{in}}$ and every $H \in \mathcal{R}_I(H_{\mathit{in}})$,
  $\Poss^I_{H_{\mathit{in}}}(H) = \Poss_{H_{\mathit{in}}}(H)$.

  \par\medskip\noindent\emph{Sufficiency.}
  Assume $\Spec$ is monotone.
  Define $\mathcal{R}_I(H_{\mathit{in}}) \triangleq
  \mathcal{A}(H_{\mathit{in}})$ and set
  $\Expose_I(H) = \Obs(H)$ for all $H$.
  Correctness holds: $\Expose_I(H) = \Obs(H) \subseteq \Obs(H)$.
  For possibility preservation: since $\mathcal{R}_I = \mathcal{A}$,
  every admissible extension is realized, and since
  $\Expose_I(H) = \Obs(H)$, every specification-permitted outcome is
  exposable.
  Hence $\Poss^I_{H_{\mathit{in}}}(H) = \Poss_{H_{\mathit{in}}}(H)$.
  Monotonicity ensures safety: for every exposed $o \in \Obs(H)$ and
  every extension $H \hext H'$, there exists $o' \in \Obs(H')$ with
  $o \Ord o'$, so no exposed outcome is ever contradicted.

  \par\medskip\noindent\emph{Necessity.}
  Assume $\Spec$ is not monotone.
  Then there exist $H_1 \hext H_2$ and $o_1 \in \Obs(H_1)$ such that
  for every $o' \in \Obs(H_2)$, $o_1$ and $o'$ contradict
  (no common refinement under $\Ord$).

  Since $\In(H_1) \hext H_1 \hext H_2$, both $H_1$ and $H_2$ lie in
  $\mathcal{A}(\In(H_1))$, and $o_1 \in \Poss_{\In(H_1)}(H_1)$.

  Suppose for contradiction that a coordination-free implementation $I$
  exists.
  Then $\Poss^I_{\In(H_1)}(H_1) = \Poss_{\In(H_1)}(H_1)$.
  If $H_2 \notin \mathcal{R}_I(\In(H_1))$, then outcomes arising only
  along $H_2$ would be absent from $\Poss^I_{\In(H_1)}(H_1)$,
  contradicting $\Poss^I = \Poss$.
  Hence $H_2 \in \mathcal{R}_I(\In(H_1))$.

  Since $o_1 \in \Obs(H_1) \subseteq \Poss_{\In(H_1)}(H_1)
  = \Poss^I_{\In(H_1)}(H_1)$, the implementation may expose $o_1$ at
  some realizable extension of $H_1$.
  But along the admissible extension $H_2$, no outcome in $\Obs(H_2)$
  is compatible with $o_1$.
  To remain correct at $H_2$, the implementation must either:
  (a)~never expose $o_1$ (suppressing a specification-permitted outcome,
  so $\Poss^I \subsetneq \Poss$), or
  (b)~exclude $H_2$ from $\mathcal{R}_I$ (already shown to contradict
  $\Poss^I = \Poss$).
  Either way, coordination-freedom is violated.
\end{proof}

\section{Minimality of the Characterization}
\label{app:minimality}

The following proposition formalizes the claim that monotonicity
is the weakest semantic property guaranteeing coordination-freedom.

\begin{proposition}[Semantic minimality]
  \label{prop:minimality}
  Let $\Spec=(E,\Obs,\Ord)$ admit a coordination-free implementation.
  Then there exists a partial order $\Ord^*$ on $O$, induced by
  observable compatibility under that implementation, such that $\Spec$
  is monotone with respect to $\Ord^*$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  Let $I$ be a coordination-free implementation.
  Define $o_1 \rightsquigarrow o_2$ iff there exist histories
  $H_1 \hext H_2$ with $\In(H_1) = \In(H_2)$ such that
  $o_1 \in \Expose_I(H_1)$ and $o_2 \in \Expose_I(H_2)$.
  Let $\Ord^*$ be the reflexive--transitive closure of
  $\rightsquigarrow$, quotiented by mutual reachability.

  Because $I$ is coordination-free, for every $H_1$ and every
  admissible extension $H_2 \hext H_1$, $\Poss^I = \Poss$.
  Hence for each $o_1 \in \Expose_I(H_1)$ and each extension $H_2$,
  there exists $o_2 \in \Expose_I(H_2)$ with $o_1 \Ord^* o_2$.
  Thus $\Spec$ is monotone under $\Ord^*$.
\end{proof}

\noindent
The order $\Ord^*$ is derived entirely from observable behavior.
It captures exactly those outcome transitions that remain compatible
under extension.
Any semantic criterion sufficient for coordination-freedom must entail
monotonicity under some such induced order.
Monotonicity is therefore the weakest semantic structure that
coordination-free behavior can exhibit.

\section{Additional Applications}
\label{app:additional}

This appendix includes applications from distributed computing that
demonstrate the breadth of Complete CALM beyond the data-management
focus of the body.

\subsection{Consensus and Authority}
Agreement protocols decompose into authority closure (non-monotone:
committing to a leader may be invalidated by a concurrent extension)
and value exposure under fixed authority (monotone: decisions only
accumulate).
The authority phase requires coordination; the value phase is
well-coordinated.
This decomposition directly illustrates the well-coordination concept:
after authority is established, the system is well-coordinated for
value decisions.
A full formalization appears in the extended
version~\cite{hellerstein2026coordinationcriterion}.

\subsection{Snapshots}
Global snapshots (downward-closed cuts of happens-before) are
monotone: new events appear only after the cut.
Atomic snapshots (committing to a linearization point) are not:
a concurrent extension can introduce an event that must be placed
between previously adjacent operations.
The partial-order/total-order distinction from
Section~\ref{sec:isolation} applies here as well.

\subsection{Agreement Tasks}
$k$-set agreement and strong renaming are non-monotone for structural
reasons (bounding decision diversity, membership-sensitive namespace).
These demonstrate that weakening a coordination-requiring specification
does not necessarily restore monotonicity.
