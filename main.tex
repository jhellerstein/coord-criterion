\title{Complete CALM: Coordination Analysis for Specifications}

\author{Joseph M. Hellerstein}
\affiliation{%
  \institution{UC Berkeley \& Amazon Web Services}
  \city{Berkeley, CA}
  \country{USA}
}

\begin{abstract}
  The CALM theorem connects coordination-freedom to monotonicity, but
  its proof is tied to relational transducers and set-inclusion
  growth.
  Four lines of work have characterized coordination-freedom in
  specific models---CALM (Datalog), CRDTs (join-semilattices),
  I-confluence (transactions), and HATs (isolation levels)---without
  a unifying account.

  We show that all four are instances of a single semantic condition.
  The proof needs only a specification mapping execution histories to
  outcome sets under a declared refinement order.
  This yields \emph{Complete CALM}: a specification admits
  coordination-free implementation iff its outcomes are monotone---iff
  the physical growth of exposed observations (which can only
  accumulate) always aligns with the semantic refinement order on
  outcomes.
  The generalization provides both broader scope (any concurrent
  system, any refinement order, abstract properties without programs)
  and deeper analytical power: verification of
  proper coordination, direct analysis of ordered properties like
  linearizability, and a \emph{coordination-free frontier}
  construction that derives natural coordination-free
  weakenings for any specification---illustrated with
  registers, queues, and search structures.
  In the distributed setting, a complete version of the CAP theorem
  follows as a corollary: a specification admits a consistent,
  available, partition-tolerant implementation iff it is
  distributed-monotone---monotone with respect to remote-only futures.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% ================================================================

Coordination---consensus, atomic commit, locking---adds latency,
complexity, and fragility to concurrent systems.
When is it necessary?
The question is operational, but the answer should be semantic---a
property of \emph{what the system promises} (its specification),
not of how it is implemented.

Several disjoint answers exist.
The CALM theorem characterizes coordination-freedom for
Datalog~\cite{hellerstein2010declarative,ameloot2013relational};
I-confluence does so for transactional
invariants~\cite{bailis2014coordination}; HATs for isolation
levels~\cite{bailis2013hat}; CRDTs for replicated
objects~\cite{shapiro2011crdt}.
Each is tied to a particular computational model; none subsumes
the others.
The CAP theorem~\cite{brewer2000towards,gilbert2002cap} lacks a
formal connection to any of them~\cite{hellerstein2020keeping}.
Moreover, the transducer formulation ties coordination-freedom to
monotone growth of a common output relation; it does not directly
express coordination-free but non-convergent interfaces such as causal
consistency~\cite{bailis2013hat}.

Each prior result started from semantic constraints within a specific
model---monotone Datalog, join-semilattices, merge-closed
invariants---and proved that those constraints ensure certain
operational guarantees.
We reverse the direction: start by formalizing the operational
guarantee (safe exposure without waiting), which naturally entails a
semantic constraint that is fully model-free.

Three ingredients suffice: execution histories, admissible outcome
sets, and a declared refinement order.
A \emph{specification} maps histories to outcomes under a partial
order; we call it \emph{monotone} if every admissible outcome at
every history has a compatible refinement at every future.
The main result (\emph{Complete CALM}): a specification admits a
correct coordination-free implementation---one in which every process
responds to every invocation without waiting for any remote
information---if and only if it is monotone.
The proof constructs a concrete protocol for the sufficiency direction
and uses an indistinguishability argument (in the style of
Fischer--Lynch--Paterson and Gilbert--Lynch) for necessity.

The result is stated and proved in the standard I/O automaton
model~\cite{lynch1996distributed}: processes are I/O automata under
adversarial asynchronous scheduling, and coordination-freedom means
that after an invocation, the process can produce a response using
only internal and output actions, without requiring any further input.
This operational grounding distinguishes Complete CALM from a purely
definitional equivalence: the theorem asserts the existence of a
distributed protocol that is correct under all adversarial schedules.

This generality yields two classes of new results:

\emph{Verification of proper coordination.}
Because the criterion operates on specifications rather than programs,
it can verify that a non-monotone specification has been
\emph{resolved} by coordination---that the residual output interface
is monotone.
Existing model-specific formalisms do not provide this test: they
analyze programs within a fixed model rather than arbitrary residual
output specifications.
The criterion also handles refinement orders beyond set inclusion
(prefix extension, lattice orders) and decouples coordination-freedom
from replica consistency (Section~\ref{sec:replica-consistency}).

\emph{A proof technique for abstract properties.}
The criterion operates on specifications, not programs, directly
answering questions like ``does serializability require coordination?''
It unifies the results above under a single test and covers classical
concurrent specifications (snapshots, $k$-set agreement, strong
renaming) via the coordination-free frontier
(Appendix~\ref{app:minimality}).
Its most striking application is \emph{Complete CAP}
(Section~\ref{sec:complete-cap}): a specification admits a consistent,
available, partition-tolerant distributed implementation iff it is
distributed-monotone---lifting CAP from a one-directional impossibility
about linearizability to a bidirectional characterization.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \emph{Complete CALM} (Section~\ref{sec:complete-calm}):
        a specification admits a correct coordination-free
        implementation (in the I/O automaton model) iff it is
        monotone.
        The proof constructs a protocol for sufficiency and uses an
        indistinguishability argument for necessity.
        The criterion handles any refinement order (prefix extension,
        lattice orders), not only set inclusion.
  \item \emph{Complete CAP} (Section~\ref{sec:complete-cap}):
        in the asynchronous distributed setting, a specification
        admits a consistent, available, partition-tolerant
        implementation iff it is distributed-monotone---a
        bidirectional generalization of Gilbert--Lynch.
  \item \emph{Proper coordination}
        (Section~\ref{sec:separation}): Complete CALM verifies that
        coordination correctly discharges non-monotone specifications.
  \item \emph{Instantiation}
        (Sections~\ref{sec:calm-instance} and~\ref{sec:applications}):
        subsumes relational-transducer CALM (including the
        $N_1$/$N_2$/$N_3$ hierarchy) and recovers boundaries for
        isolation levels, I-confluence, and CRDTs.
  \item \emph{The coordination-free frontier}
        (Appendix~\ref{app:minimality}): for a fixed observation
        interface, minimal monotone enlargements of the declared order
        characterize the strongest coordination-free guarantees.
        More generally, weakening the interface itself yields
        coordination-free alternatives.
        Recovers causal consistency for
        registers~\cite{mahajan2011consistency}; yields new results
        for queues (causal FIFO) and search structures
        (forward-reachability / B-link
        trees~\cite{lehman1981efficient}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Running Example: A Replicated Register}
\label{sec:example}
% ================================================================

Consider a register replicated at two nodes, each accepting local
read and write operations and propagating updates via messages.
We define two specifications over the same histories that differ in
their outcome structure and refinement order.

\paragraph{Histories.}
A history is a partially ordered set of events ordered by Lamport's
\emph{happens-before} relation~\cite{lamport1978time}---also called
\emph{causal precedence}: $e_1$ happens before $e_2$ if $e_1$
precedes $e_2$ in program order at the same node, or $e_1$ is a
message send and $e_2$ is the corresponding receive, or transitively.
Events not related by happens-before are \emph{concurrent}.
We write $\mathsf{w}(v)$ for a write of value $v$,
$\mathsf{r} \mapsto v$ for a read returning $v$, and
$\mathit{send}(x)$/$\mathit{recv}(x)$ for the send and receive of a
propagation message carrying $x$.

\paragraph{Two specifications.}
We write outcomes as sequences of completed operations.
%
$\Spec_{\mathit{cc}}$ (\textbf{causal
consistency}~\cite{ahamad1995causal}): each process observes a
\emph{causal history}---a partial order on the operations in its
causal past, consistent with happens-before.
Different processes may order concurrent writes differently.
A read returns a value consistent with some linearization of its
causal past.
Refinement is \emph{causal-prefix extension}: $o_1 \Ord o_2$ iff
$o_2$ extends $o_1$ by adding operations while preserving existing
order.
%
$\Spec_{\mathit{lin}}$
(\textbf{linearizability}~\cite{herlihy1990linearizability}): there
exists a \emph{single} total order on all completed operations,
consistent with happens-before (i.e., if operation $A$'s response
causally precedes operation $B$'s invocation, then $A$ precedes $B$
in the total order), with reads returning the most recent preceding
write.
In the asynchronous message-passing model, happens-before subsumes
real-time precedence: if $A$ completes before $B$ begins at the same
or a causally connected process, happens-before records this.
This is the standard atomic-register semantics used by Gilbert and
Lynch~\cite{gilbert2002cap}.
Refinement is \emph{prefix extension}: $o_1 \Ord o_2$ iff $o_1$ is a
prefix of $o_2$.

The two specifications share the same histories but differ in outcome
structure: CC allows per-process views that disagree on the order of
concurrent writes; linearizability demands a single global order.
This choice determines coordination requirements.

\paragraph{Witness.}
The register initially holds $0$.
Two nodes $p$ and $q$ each write concurrently:
$\mathsf{w}_p(1)$ at $p$, $\mathsf{w}_q(2)$ at $q$.
The writes are concurrent (neither happens-before the other).

History $H_1$: both writes complete; $\mathsf{w}_q$ is delivered to
$p$ ($\mathit{recv}(\mathsf{w}_q)$ at $p$); $p$ then completes a
read $r_p \mapsto 2$.
The delivery creates happens-before:
$\mathsf{w}_q \rightarrow \mathit{send} \rightarrow
\mathit{recv} \rightarrow r_p$, so $\mathsf{w}_q$ precedes $r_p$.
A linearizable outcome at $H_1$:
\[
  o_1 = \langle \mathsf{w}_p(1),\; \mathsf{w}_q(2),\;
  r_p{\,\mapsto}2 \rangle.
\]
This respects happens-before ($\mathsf{w}_q$ before $r_p$; the two
writes are concurrent, so either order is valid) and explains
$r_p = 2$ (most recent write before $r_p$ is $\mathsf{w}_q$).

Extend to $H_2$: deliver $\mathsf{w}_p$ to $q$
($\mathit{recv}(\mathsf{w}_p)$ at $q$); $q$ then completes a read
$r_q \mapsto 1$.
This is a valid future of $H_1$: only causally later events are
added.
Now happens-before at $H_2$ also has
$\mathsf{w}_p \rightarrow \mathit{send} \rightarrow
\mathit{recv} \rightarrow r_q$, so $\mathsf{w}_p$ precedes $r_q$.

Under $\Spec_{\mathit{lin}}$, the specification is not monotone.
Any linearization at $H_2$ explaining $r_p \mapsto 2$ must have
$\mathsf{w}_p < \mathsf{w}_q$ (so $\mathsf{w}_q$ is the most recent
write before $r_p$).
But any linearization explaining $r_q \mapsto 1$ must have
$\mathsf{w}_q < \mathsf{w}_p$ (so $\mathsf{w}_p$ is the most recent
write before $r_q$).
These force $\mathsf{w}_p < \mathsf{w}_q$ and
$\mathsf{w}_q < \mathsf{w}_p$---a contradiction.
No linearizable outcome at $H_2$ extends $o_1$.
Non-monotonicity: $o_1 \in \Obs(H_1)$ but
$\nexists\, o' \in \Obs(H_2) : o_1 \Ord o'$.
The operational consequence: before committing to the order
$\mathsf{w}_p < \mathsf{w}_q$ (by responding $r_p \mapsto 2$),
process $p$ must ensure that $q$ will not independently commit to the
opposite order---this requires coordination.

Under $\Spec_{\mathit{cc}}$, the specification is monotone.
At $H_1$, process $p$ observes the causal view
$\mathsf{w}_p < \mathsf{w}_q < r_p$, returning $2$.
At $H_2$, process $q$ observes the causal view
$\mathsf{w}_q < \mathsf{w}_p < r_q$, returning $1$.
The two views order the concurrent writes differently---but CC permits
this: concurrent writes need not be placed in a single global order.
The outcome at $H_2$ refines the outcome at $H_1$ by adding another
causally valid view, rather than contradicting the first.
Every outcome at $H_1$ has a refinement at every future.
No coordination is needed.

The two specifications separate cleanly: $\Spec_{\mathit{cc}}$ is
coordination-free; $\Spec_{\mathit{lin}}$ requires coordination.
The same analysis applies to concurrent threads on a single
machine---the criterion depends on the happens-before structure, not
the mechanism of concurrency.

\section{Framework}
\label{sec:model}
% ================================================================

We model concurrent executions as Lamport histories and define
specifications, implementations, and coordination-freedom in terms of
observable outcomes.
The goal is to isolate a single semantic question: when can the
physical accumulation of exposed outcomes align with the declared
refinement order without suppressing admissible futures?

The framework applies to any concurrent setting---distributed or local.
Coordination has costs in both: locking and abort in a local database,
latency and partition vulnerability in a distributed system.
The criterion characterizes when these costs are \emph{intrinsic} to the
specification (unavoidable under any implementation) versus
\emph{incidental} (artifacts of a particular implementation strategy).
In the distributed case, establishing membership and a consistent
input-ordering authority isolates all coordination into a single
reusable layer; downstream evaluation of the ordered stream is
coordination-free (Appendix~\ref{app:universal}).

We build toward the criterion in three steps: first the execution model
(histories and their causal growth), then the specification language
(what outcomes are admissible and how they relate), and finally the
implementation model (what it means to be coordination-free).

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Histories and Futures}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

We represent concurrent executions as partially ordered sets of events,
following Lamport~\cite{lamport1978time}.
The partial order captures causal precedence; concurrent events have no
ordering.

\begin{definition}[Event universe]
  A specification fixes pairwise-disjoint sets of events:
  $E_{\mathit{in}}$ (client interface: invocations and responses),
  $E_{\mathit{int}}$ (internal computation),
  $E_{\mathit{send}}$ (message sends),
  $E_{\mathit{recv}}$ (message receives).
\end{definition}

\noindent
The disjoint sets distinguish interface activity from internal
computation and message-passing structure.
$E_{\mathit{in}}$ contains client-facing \emph{interface events}:
both invocations and responses (the observable input/output of the
system as seen by clients).
In the I/O automaton model of Section~\ref{sec:operational},
invocations are input actions and responses are output actions; the
history records both as interface events regardless of which party
initiates them.
In the register example, $E_{\mathit{in}}$ contains invocations
$\mathit{inv}(w)$, $\mathit{inv}(r)$ and responses
$\mathit{resp}(w)$, $\mathit{resp}(r, v)$;
$E_{\mathit{int}}$ contains local state updates;
$E_{\mathit{send}}$ and $E_{\mathit{recv}}$ contain inter-replica
propagation messages.

\begin{definition}[History]
  \label{def:history}
  A \emph{history} is a pair $H = (E, \rightarrow)$ where
  $E \subseteq E_{\mathit{in}} \cup E_{\mathit{int}} \cup
  E_{\mathit{send}} \cup E_{\mathit{recv}}$,
  $\rightarrow$ is a strict partial order on $E$, and
  there is an injective matching function
  $\mu : E \cap E_{\mathit{recv}} \hookrightarrow
  E \cap E_{\mathit{send}}$,
  with $\mu(r) \rightarrow r$ for every receive $r$
  (each send precedes its matching receive).
  A history is \emph{well-formed} if it satisfies these constraints.
  We write $\Hist$ for the set of well-formed histories over $E$.
\end{definition}

\noindent
A history need not be complete: it may represent a prefix with pending
operations or in-flight messages.
Messages may be delayed, reordered, or lost (a send without a matching
receive denotes a lost or indefinitely delayed message).
We impose no fairness or progress assumptions.

\begin{example}[Register history $H_1$]
  \label{ex:h1-formal}
  The history $H_1$ from Section~\ref{sec:example} in the formal
  model: at $p$, $\mathit{inv}(w_p), \mathit{resp}(w_p) \in
  E_{\mathit{in}}$ (write of $1$ complete);
  at $q$, $\mathit{inv}(w_q), \mathit{resp}(w_q) \in E_{\mathit{in}}$
  (write of $2$ complete);
  $\mathit{send}(w_q) \in E_{\mathit{send}}$ and
  $\mathit{recv}(w_q) \in E_{\mathit{recv}}$ at $p$ (delivery of
  $w_q$ to $p$);
  $\mathit{inv}(r_p), \mathit{resp}(r_p, 2) \in E_{\mathit{in}}$
  (read at $p$ returning $2$, after receiving $w_q$).
  Ordering includes
  $\mathit{resp}(w_q) \rightarrow \mathit{send}(w_q) \rightarrow
  \mathit{recv}(w_q) \rightarrow \mathit{inv}(r_p)$
  and program order $\mathit{resp}(w_p) \rightarrow \mathit{inv}(r_p)$
  at $p$.
\end{example}

As an execution unfolds, new events occur.
A \emph{future} of a history preserves all existing events and their
causal order, adding only causally later events:

\begin{definition}[Future]
  \label{def:future}
  For $H_1 = (E_1, \rightarrow_1)$ and $H_2 = (E_2, \rightarrow_2)$,
  we write $H_1 \hext H_2$ (\emph{$H_2$ is a future of $H_1$}) if:
  \begin{quote}
  \noindent
  $(i)\; E_1 \subseteq E_2$ \qquad
  $(ii)\; {\rightarrow_1} = {\rightarrow_2} \cap (E_1 \times E_1)$\\[2pt]
  $(iii)\; E_1$ is downward-closed under $\rightarrow_2$:
  if $e \in E_1$ and $e' \rightarrow_2 e$, then $e' \in E_1$.
  \end{quote}
\end{definition}

\noindent
Condition (iii) is the key constraint: a future can add causally
\emph{later} events but cannot insert new predecessors of existing ones.
The past is fixed; only the future is open.

\begin{example}[Extending $H_1$ to $H_2$]
  \label{ex:h2-formal}
  Extend $H_1$ by delivering $w_p$ to $q$:
  add $\mathit{send}(w_p) \in E_{\mathit{send}}$ at $p$ and
  $\mathit{recv}(w_p) \in E_{\mathit{recv}}$ at $q$
  ($\mathit{resp}(w_p) \rightarrow \mathit{send}(w_p) \rightarrow
  \mathit{recv}(w_p)$);
  then add a completed read at $q$:
  $\mathit{inv}(r_q), \mathit{resp}(r_q, 1) \in E_{\mathit{in}}$
  with $\mathit{recv}(w_p) \rightarrow \mathit{inv}(r_q)$ and
  program order $\mathit{resp}(w_q) \rightarrow \mathit{inv}(r_q)$
  at $q$.
  All new events are causally after existing events only---no new
  predecessor of any event in $H_1$ is introduced.
  The result: $w_p$ is causally prior to $r_q$ (via delivery), and
  $w_q$ is causally prior to $r_p$ (already in $H_1$).
\end{example}

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Specifications}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

A specification says which outcomes are admissible at each history and
how outcomes relate to one another under refinement.
It is a purely semantic object: it constrains what may be observed, not
how executions unfold.

\begin{definition}[Specification]
  \label{def:spec}
  A \emph{specification} is a triple $\Spec = (E, \Obs, \Ord)$ where:
  \begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item $E$ is an event universe fixing the space of histories $\Hist$,
    \item $\Obs : \Hist \to \mathcal{P}(O)$ maps each history to a
          set of admissible \emph{outcomes} (possibly empty for
          unrealizable histories),
    \item $\Ord$ is a partial order on outcomes, where $o_1 \Ord o_2$
          means $o_2$ \emph{refines} $o_1$ without contradicting it.
  \end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\noindent
$\Obs(H)$ is the set of outcomes that the specification permits at
$H$: what an implementation may expose, including return values for
operations whose invocation is present but whose response is not yet.
$\Obs(H) = \emptyset$ signals that $H$ is unrealizable---no correct
implementation may produce it (used for safety specifications such as
invariant enforcement; see Section~\ref{sec:i-confluence}).
Multiple outcomes at a given history reflect semantic flexibility
(multiple valid answers); any concrete execution exposes at most one.

The outcome order $\Ord$ is part of the specification---it is not
derived from execution structure but declared as part of the semantic
contract.
It encodes which transitions between outcomes merely add detail (upward
in $\Ord$, compatible with prior observations) and which exclude
alternatives (incomparable or downward, contradicting prior
observations).
Different specifications over the same histories may use different
outcome orders.

\begin{remark}[Choosing the outcome order]
  The outcome order $\Ord$ is a modeling choice, but in practice it
  follows from the structure of the output.
  Typical choices:
  if the output is an ordered stream, $\Ord$ is prefix containment;
  if the output is an unordered collection, $\Ord$ is set or multiset
  containment;
  if the output is a value in a domain with a natural partial order
  (e.g., a lattice), $\Ord$ is that order.
  An order with a top element (refining everything) makes
  all specifications trivially monotone; an order recording
  irrelevant implementation details introduces spurious
  non-monotonicity.
  The outcome order is part of the specification's semantic
  contract---changing the order changes the specification.
  The applications in Section~\ref{sec:applications} illustrate natural
  choices: prefix extension for transaction logs, set inclusion for
  committed facts, and lattice order for CRDT states.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Outcomes and order for the register]
  \label{ex:register-outcomes}
  Under $\Spec_{\mathit{lin}}$, outcomes are total orders (sequences)
  of completed operations.
  At $H_1$ (Section~\ref{sec:example}), both writes have completed
  and $r_p$ has returned $2$.
  The admissible outcome is
  $o_1 = \langle \mathsf{w}_p(1), \mathsf{w}_q(2),
  r_p{\,\mapsto}2 \rangle$
  (the only linearization consistent with happens-before that explains
  $r_p = 2$).
  At $H_2$, the additional read $r_q \mapsto 1$ forces the opposite
  write order; no single linearization can explain both reads, so
  $o_1$ has no extension at $H_2$.
\end{example}

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Implementations and Coordination-Freedom}
\label{sec:implementations}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

A specification is passive: it declares which outcomes are admissible but
says nothing about which histories actually arise or which outcomes are
actually exposed.
An \emph{implementation} is active: it determines which outcomes are
exposed at each history (by choosing responses to invocations).
The environment controls which histories arise (by scheduling inputs
and message deliveries); the implementation controls how it responds.

The central question is: can an implementation preserve all of the
specification's possibilities---or must it narrow them by suppressing
futures or withholding outcomes?

\begin{definition}[Input projection]
  For a history $H = (E, \rightarrow)$, the \emph{input projection}
  $\In(H)$ restricts $H$ to its input events:
  $\In(H) = (E \cap E_{\mathit{in}},\;
  \rightarrow \cap (E_{\mathit{in}} \times E_{\mathit{in}}))$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Admissible histories]
  For an input history $H_{\mathit{in}}$ (consisting only of input
  events), the \emph{admissible histories} are all well-formed histories
  whose input projection extends $H_{\mathit{in}}$:
  \[
    \mathcal{A}(H_{\mathit{in}}) \triangleq
    \{H \in \Hist \mid H_{\mathit{in}} \hext \In(H)\}.
  \]
  These are the histories that \emph{could} arise under asynchrony and
  causality alone, given the input prefix $H_{\mathit{in}}$.
  They represent the environment's nondeterminism (message timing,
  input arrival order, scheduling) rather than implementation choices.
  Well-formedness (Definition~\ref{def:history}) already excludes
  physically impossible histories (e.g., a receive without a matching
  send); $\mathcal{A}$ contains only causally coherent executions.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Implementation]
  \label{def:implementation}
  An implementation $I$ specifies, for each input history
  $H_{\mathit{in}}$, a set of \emph{realizable histories}
  $\mathcal{R}_I(H_{\mathit{in}}) \subseteq
  \mathcal{A}(H_{\mathit{in}})$, downward-closed under $\hext$.
\end{definition}

\noindent
An implementation determines which histories can arise.
Downward-closure means every prefix of a realizable history is
realizable.
The key question is whether the implementation must suppress some
admissible histories---or withhold some admissible outcomes---to avoid
future contradictions.

\begin{definition}[Future-consistent outcome]
  \label{def:future-consistent}
  An outcome $o \in \Obs(H)$ is \emph{future-consistent} at $H$ if
  for every future $H'$ of $H$, there exists $o' \in \Obs(H')$ with
  $o \Ord o'$.
  An outcome is \emph{future-inconsistent} at $H$ if some future
  admits no such refinement.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Coordination-free implementation]
  \label{def:coordfree}
  An implementation $I$ is \emph{coordination-free} if:
  \begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\roman*)]
    \item $\mathcal{R}_I(H_{\mathit{in}}) =
          \mathcal{A}(H_{\mathit{in}})$ for all $H_{\mathit{in}}$
          \hfill(no history suppression), and
    \item every $o \in \Obs(H)$ is future-consistent at every
          $H \in \mathcal{A}(H_{\mathit{in}})$
          \hfill(no outcome suppression).
  \end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\noindent
Condition~(i) says the implementation never prevents an admissible
future from occurring.
Condition~(ii) says every admissible outcome can be safely exposed:
no outcome need be withheld to avoid future contradiction.
Together: a coordination-free implementation can act on any admissible
outcome at any history without waiting for information about which
future will materialize.
Condition~(ii) is deliberately specification-level: it says that the
interface itself admits no unsafe outcome.
Implementations enter only through history suppression---if an
implementation filters outcomes, we model the result as a coordinated
variant with a smaller $\Obs$ (Section~\ref{sec:separation}).

\begin{remark}[Why every outcome must be future-consistent]
  A weaker definition might require only that \emph{some}
  future-consistent outcome exist at each history.
  But a partitioned node cannot distinguish future-consistent outcomes
  from future-inconsistent ones without knowing which futures will
  materialize---and acquiring that knowledge is coordination.
  The universal condition requires no such oracle.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Coordination in the register]
  Under $\Spec_{\mathit{lin}}$, the outcome
  $o_1 = \langle \mathsf{w}_p(1), \mathsf{w}_q(2),
  r_p{\,\mapsto}2 \rangle$ is admissible at $H_1$, but $H_2$ is a
  future of $H_1$ and no outcome in $\Obs_{\mathit{lin}}(H_2)$
  refines it under $\Ord$.
  The $H_1$ outcome is future-inconsistent: the specification forces
  either outcome suppression or history suppression.
\end{example}

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Operational Characterization}
\label{sec:operational}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

The preceding definitions characterize coordination-freedom at the
specification level.
We now ground them in a concrete operational model---I/O
automata~\cite{lynch1996distributed}---and prove that the semantic
condition (monotonicity) exactly characterizes when a correct,
non-blocking implementation exists.

\begin{definition}[Process (I/O automaton)]
  \label{def:process}
  Each process $p_i$ is an I/O automaton with:
  \begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \emph{Input actions:} client invocations
          $\mathsf{inv}(e)_i$ and message arrivals
          $\mathsf{recv}(m)_i$.
    \item \emph{Output actions:} client responses
          $\mathsf{resp}(e, v)_i$ and message sends
          $\mathsf{send}(m)_i$.
    \item \emph{Internal actions:} local computation steps
          $\tau_i$ (state updates, gossip decisions,
          pre-computation).
    \item A state space $\Sigma_i$, initial state $\sigma_i^0$,
          and a transition relation on
          $\Sigma_i \times \mathit{acts}_i \times \Sigma_i$.
  \end{itemize}
  Input actions are always enabled (the automaton cannot refuse an
  invocation or message).
  A \emph{distributed implementation} is a composition of $n$ such
  automata communicating via shared send/receive actions.
\end{definition}

\noindent
An \emph{execution} is a (possibly infinite) alternating sequence of
states and actions, starting from initial states, consistent with the
transition relations.
The adversarial asynchronous environment controls when input actions
occur: it determines message delivery order, timing, and the arrival
of client invocations.
Internal and output actions are controlled by the automata themselves.
We assume \emph{fair local scheduling}: enabled internal and output
actions at a process are eventually executed (the environment cannot
indefinitely suppress a process's local computation).

\begin{definition}[Coordination-free (operational)]
  \label{def:coordfree-op}
  A distributed implementation is \emph{coordination-free} if for
  every process $p_i$ and every client invocation
  $\mathsf{inv}(e)_i$, the response $\mathsf{resp}(e, v)_i$ is
  \emph{enabled} immediately: there exists an execution fragment from
  $p_i$'s post-invocation state consisting only of internal and
  output actions at $p_i$ that produces $\mathsf{resp}(e, v)_i$,
  without requiring any further input action at $p_i$.
\end{definition}

\noindent
The response is determined entirely by $p_i$'s state at invocation
time.
The process may perform local computation (internal actions) before
emitting the response, but it need not---and does not---wait for any
message arrival or additional client invocation.
This captures both the distributed case (no waiting for remote
messages) and the local case (no waiting for other concurrent
invocations---e.g., no locking that defers one transaction pending
another).
Operationally: if the network goes silent and no other client
requests arrive after the invocation, the process can still respond.
This is the availability condition of Gilbert and
Lynch~\cite{gilbert2002cap} expressed in the I/O automaton framework.
We restrict to deterministic automata (internal and output actions are
determined by the current state); nondeterministic choices can be
modeled as internal events included in the state without loss of
generality for safety properties.

\begin{definition}[Correctness (operational)]
  Let $\mathsf{Resp}(H)$ denote the set of (invocation, response
  value) pairs emitted in execution prefix $H$.
  An implementation is \emph{correct} for $\Spec$ if for every
  execution prefix with induced history $H$, there exists
  $o \in \Obs(H)$ that \emph{explains} $\mathsf{Resp}(H)$: for every
  $(e, v) \in \mathsf{Resp}(H)$, outcome $o$ prescribes response $v$
  to invocation $e$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Complete CALM, operational]
  \label{thm:calm-operational}
  A specification $\Spec$ admits a correct coordination-free
  distributed implementation iff $\Spec$ is monotone.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
  \emph{Sufficiency (monotone $\Rightarrow$ implementation exists).}
  Construct the \emph{causal-view protocol}: each process maintains
  as state its local causal history $H_i$ (the events that have
  causally reached it) and propagates it via gossip (internal actions
  trigger $\mathsf{send}$).
  On $\mathsf{inv}(e)_i$, the process adds $e$ to $H_i$, computes
  $\Obs(H_i)$, chooses $o \in \Obs(H_i)$, and immediately performs
  $\mathsf{resp}(e, v)_i$ where $v$ is $o$'s prescription for $e$.
  No input action intervenes.
  (If $\Obs(H_i) = \emptyset$---the local history is
  unrealizable---the process may respond with an error/abort; this
  case does not arise for monotone specifications at reachable
  histories, since monotonicity propagates nonemptiness forward.)

  \emph{Correctness:} At any prefix with global history $H$, each
  local view satisfies $H_i \hext H$.
  By monotonicity, the chosen $o \in \Obs(H_i)$ has a refinement
  $o' \in \Obs(H)$ with $o \Ord o'$.
  Since refinement preserves earlier responses, the emitted response
  is consistent with $o'$.

\begin{remark}[Joint consistency of independent responses]
  \label{rem:joint-consistency}
  Joint consistency of responses emitted by different processes does
  not require a separate assumption.
  Once a process emits a response, that response event enters the
  history and constrains all future admissible outcomes.
  A later process responding at a history that includes the earlier
  response must choose an outcome explaining both---and monotonicity
  guarantees such an outcome exists (the earlier outcome refines into
  the later history).
  The causal-view protocol achieves this automatically: gossip
  propagates response events, so each process's local view includes
  prior responses from other processes before it responds to new
  invocations.
\end{remark}

  \emph{Necessity (coordination-free $\Rightarrow$ monotone).}
  Contrapositive: if $\Spec$ is not monotone, no correct
  implementation is coordination-free.
  Let $o \in \Obs(H_1)$ be future-inconsistent, witnessed by
  $H_1 \hext H_2$, with $o$ prescribing response $v$ to invocation
  $e$ at process $p$.
  The proof requires that $H_2$ be realizable as a \emph{$p$-silent
  future}: one where the events in $H_2 \setminus H_1$ do not include
  input actions at $p$ (so $p$ cannot distinguish $H_1$ from $H_2$
  before responding).
  This holds whenever the non-monotonicity witness involves activity
  at processes other than $p$---the standard case for distributed
  specifications.
  (For purely process-local non-monotonicity, where the dangerous
  future requires new input at $p$ itself, $p$ can observe the danger
  and react; such non-monotonicity does not prevent coordination-free
  implementation.
  This is the same distinction captured by distributed-monotonicity in
  Section~\ref{sec:complete-cap}.)

  Construct two executions with identical state at $p$ when $e$
  arrives:
  \begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Execution $\alpha$: global history is $H_1$; after $e$
          arrives at $p$, no further input actions occur at $p$
          (messages delayed, no new invocations).
    \item Execution $\beta$: same local state at $p$ when $e$
          arrives, but other processes advance to produce $H_2$
          (a $p$-silent future---no input actions at $p$).
  \end{itemize}
  Since $p$'s state is identical and the automaton is deterministic
  on internal/output actions given the state, $p$ produces the same
  response $v$ in both (coordination-freedom guarantees the response
  is enabled without further input).
  In execution $\alpha$, $v$ is correct (consistent with
  $o \in \Obs(H_1)$).
  In execution $\beta$, the global history is $H_2$ and no
  $o' \in \Obs(H_2)$ refines $o$---correctness is violated.
  Hence no correct implementation is coordination-free.
\end{proof}

\noindent
The semantic characterization (Definition~\ref{def:coordfree}) is a
corollary: condition~(i) corresponds to the environment's power to
produce any admissible execution, and condition~(ii) corresponds to
the process responding without knowing which future will materialize.

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Complete CALM}
\label{sec:complete-calm}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

We can now state the criterion precisely.
The question is: when does a specification force every correct
implementation to suppress admissible futures?
The answer is a single semantic property of the specification.

\begin{definition}[Monotonicity of a specification]
  \label{def:monotone}
  A specification $\Spec = (E, \Obs, \Ord)$ is \emph{monotone} if
  for all $H_1, H_2 \in \Hist$ with $H_1 \hext H_2$ and all
  $o \in \Obs(H_1)$, there exists $o' \in \Obs(H_2)$ with $o \Ord o'$.
\end{definition}

\noindent
This generalizes CALM's notion of monotonicity from a specific mapping
(input facts $\to$ output facts, ordered by set inclusion) to an
arbitrary one (histories $\to$ outcome sets, ordered by any
user-defined refinement $\Ord$).
The definition is the same---outputs refine as inputs grow---but the
domain, range, and order are all generalized.

Monotonicity is the specification-level characterization of future
consistency: once an outcome is admitted at a history, every future
admits a compatible refinement.
Equivalently: a specification is monotone iff every admissible outcome
is future-consistent at every history.

A failure of monotonicity is witnessed by a
\emph{future-inconsistent outcome}: an outcome $o \in \Obs(H_1)$ such
that some future $H_2$ of $H_1$ admits no $o' \in \Obs(H_2)$ with
$o \Ord o'$.
The register witness from Section~\ref{sec:example} is exactly such a
failure: $o_1 = \langle \mathsf{w}_p(1), \mathsf{w}_q(2),
r_p{\,\mapsto}2 \rangle$ has no prefix-extension at $H_2$.

Prior work on CALM requires involved proofs mapping from
programming-model constraints to execution properties.
Complete CALM separates the semantic characterization from the
operational content (which requires a protocol construction and an
impossibility argument, Theorem~\ref{thm:calm-operational} above):

\begin{theorem}[Complete CALM]
  \label{thm:complete-calm}
  The following are equivalent for a specification
  $\Spec = (E, \Obs, \Ord)$:
  \begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\roman*)]
    \item $\Spec$ admits a correct coordination-free implementation
          (in the I/O automaton model of
          Section~\ref{sec:operational}).
    \item $\Spec$ is monotone.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{example}[Applying Complete CALM to the running example]
  $\Spec_{\mathit{cc}}$ is monotone: each process's causal view
  remains valid under all extensions---concurrent writes may be
  perceived in different orders at different processes, and no future
  delivery contradicts an earlier perception.
  By Complete CALM, causal consistency admits a coordination-free
  implementation.

  $\Spec_{\mathit{lin}}$ is not monotone (the witness from
  Section~\ref{sec:example}).
  By Complete CALM, every correct implementation of linearizability
  must coordinate---it must suppress some admissible
  future or withhold some future-inconsistent outcome.
  Section~\ref{sec:well-coord-section} characterizes when such
  coordination suffices to restore monotonicity of the resolved
  specification.
\end{example}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
  \emph{Sufficiency.}
  If $\Spec$ is monotone, every outcome is future-consistent
  (directly from the definitions).
  Set $\mathcal{R}_I = \mathcal{A}$: both conditions of
  coordination-freedom are satisfied.

  \emph{Necessity.}
  If $\Spec$ is not monotone, some $o_1 \in \Obs(H_1)$ is
  future-inconsistent (witnessed by $H_1 \hext H_2$ with no
  refinement of $o_1$ at $H_2$).
  Condition~(ii) fails: the specification is not coordination-free.
\end{proof}

\noindent
The characterization is tight within this model: any specification
with even one future-inconsistent outcome requires coordination
(no weaker condition suffices); conversely, every monotone
specification admits a coordination-free implementation (no stronger
condition is implied).

The result recovers the structure of Ameloot et al.'s characterization
in a minimalist setting (histories, outcomes, refinement order)---the
proof needs only these three ingredients.
The operational content (Theorem~\ref{thm:calm-operational}) is that
this semantic condition exactly characterizes when a concrete
distributed protocol exists that is correct under all adversarial
schedules.
This lifting yields three dividends.
First, \emph{generality}: the criterion applies to any specification
in any computational model.
Second, \emph{expressivity}: refinement orders beyond set inclusion
(prefix extension, lattice orders) are handled directly
(Section~\ref{sec:beyond-calm}).
Third, \emph{constructive power}: for any non-monotone specification,
the \emph{coordination-free frontier}---the set of minimal monotone
enlargements of $\Ord$---characterizes the strongest coordination-free
guarantees achievable for a fixed observation interface
(Appendix~\ref{app:minimality}).
The frontier recovers causal consistency for
registers~\cite{mahajan2011consistency}, derives causal FIFO for
queues, and derives forward-reachability (B-link
trees~\cite{lehman1981efficient}) for search structures.

Since the criterion operates on semantic specifications, checking
monotonicity is undecidable in general (analogous to Rice's theorem).
However, conservative (sufficient) checks based on type-level
monotonicity annotations are practical and effective.

\section{Proper Coordination}
\label{sec:well-coord-section}
% ================================================================

The most distinctive capability of Complete CALM beyond the original
is \emph{verification of proper coordination}:
it can verify that coordination \emph{correctly discharges}
non-monotonicity, leaving a monotone output interface for downstream
consumers.
We call this \emph{proper coordination}.

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Coordinated Variants}
\label{sec:separation}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

The central insight is simple: coordination is a \emph{means}, not an
\emph{end}.
A system may use coordination internally to resolve a non-monotone
specification, producing a monotone output interface for downstream
consumers.
A coordinated variant always exists (the universal construction of
Appendix~\ref{app:universal} provides one for any spec); the question
is how to verify that a \emph{given} variant is properly coordinated.

\begin{definition}[Properly coordinated variant]
  \label{def:coord-variant}
  A specification $\Spec' = (E, \Obs', \Ord)$ is a \emph{properly
  coordinated variant} of $\Spec = (E, \Obs, \Ord)$ if:
  \begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\roman*)]
    \item $\Obs'(H) \subseteq \Obs(H)$ for all $H$
          (no new outcomes admitted), and
    \item $\Spec'$ is monotone.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\noindent
A properly coordinated variant restricts the original spec---fewer
admissible outcomes, fewer admissible histories, or both---just enough
to achieve monotonicity.
Complete CALM provides the test: check whether the variant is
monotone.
If it is, the coordination suffices.
If not, more restriction is needed.

\begin{theorem}[Separation from relational-transducer CALM]
  \label{thm:separation}
  There exist non-monotone specifications with properly coordinated
  variants that Complete CALM can verify but relational-transducer
  CALM has no mechanism to certify (it lacks a semantic test for
  the output specification of a coordinated variant).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
  Linearizability is non-monotone
  (Section~\ref{sec:example}).
  Define the variant $\Spec'$ by restricting to histories in which
  $\rightarrow$ totally orders $E_{\mathit{in}}$ (all input operations
  are serialized---no two are concurrent).
  In such histories, outcomes grow by prefix extension of the
  serialized input sequence, so $\Spec'$ is monotone.
  Complete CALM certifies $\Spec'$ directly.
  Relational-transducer CALM cannot: standard Datalog encodings of
  linearizability require a non-monotone uniqueness constraint to
  enforce that each position in the sequence has exactly one value
  (Section~\ref{sec:beyond-calm}), and the transducer model has no
  mechanism to test the output specification of a coordinated variant
  independently of the program that produces it.
  (The gap is not that CALM gives a wrong answer but that it answers a
  different question: whether the \emph{program} is monotone, not
  whether the \emph{output interface} is coordination-free.)
\end{proof}

\noindent
The separation is the shift from coordination-\emph{avoiding}
programs to properly \emph{coordinated} specifications: Complete CALM
can verify that coordination suffices, not merely that it is absent.


% ================================================================
\section{Complete CAP}
\label{sec:complete-cap}
% ================================================================

Complete CALM characterizes when coordination is required---a semantic
property independent of deployment model.
In the distributed setting, a natural conjecture is that the CAP
tradeoff coincides with non-monotonicity: a specification forces a
choice between consistency, availability, and partition tolerance iff
it is non-monotone.
This is almost right, but requires one refinement: some
non-monotonicity is process-local and can be resolved without
communication (e.g., via a local mutex).
Only non-monotonicity that \emph{spans} a partition---where remote
activity can invalidate a local outcome---creates the CAP dilemma.
Getting this exclusion right is the main technical contribution of
this section.

\paragraph{Process assignment.}
We extend the framework with a \emph{process assignment}
$\pi : E \to \{p_1, \ldots, p_n\}$ mapping each event to the process
at which it occurs.
This is part of the specification's interface contract (e.g., ``reads
at the local replica,'' ``writes at the primary'').

\begin{definition}[Partition-constrained future]
  \label{def:partition-future}
  Fix a partition $P = (S, \bar{S})$ of the process set with
  $S \cap \bar{S} = \emptyset$ and
  $S \cup \bar{S} = \{p_1, \ldots, p_n\}$.
  A future $H_2 \sqsupseteq H_1$ is \emph{$P$-constrained} if
  $H_2 \setminus H_1$ contains no message receive whose matching send
  is on the other side of $P$.
  Both sides may continue receiving inputs, computing, sending
  messages, and delivering messages within their own side---only
  cross-partition delivery is blocked.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Distributed-monotone]
  \label{def:dist-monotone}
  A specification $\Spec = (E, \Obs, \Ord)$ with process assignment
  $\pi$ is \emph{distributed-monotone} if for every partition
  $P = (S, \bar{S})$ with $|S| \geq 1$ and $|\bar{S}| \geq 1$,
  every process $p \in S$, every history $H_1$, every
  $o \in \Obs(H_1)$ exposed at $p$, and every $P$-constrained future
  $H_2$ of $H_1$, there exists $o' \in \Obs(H_2)$ with $o \Ord o'$.
  An outcome is \emph{exposed at $p$} if $p$ is the process that
  would communicate it to a client---formally, if $o$ determines a
  response to an invocation event at $p$.
\end{definition}

\noindent
Monotonicity implies distributed-monotonicity (all futures includes
partition-constrained ones), but not conversely: a specification whose
non-monotonicity witnesses are all process-local is
distributed-monotone because no partition can produce the dangerous
future.

\begin{theorem}[Complete CAP]
  \label{cor:cap}
  In the asynchronous message-passing model with two or more
  processes, a specification admits a consistent, available,
  partition-tolerant implementation if and only if it is
  distributed-monotone.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
  ($\Leftarrow$) If distributed-monotone, every outcome at every
  process survives all partition-constrained futures.
  Under a partition, only such futures can occur.
  Hence every process can safely expose any admissible outcome without
  waiting---both availability and consistency hold.

  ($\Rightarrow$) If not distributed-monotone, there exists a
  partition $P = (S, \bar{S})$, a process $p \in S$, an outcome
  $o \in \Obs(H_1)$ at $p$, and a $P$-constrained future $H_2$ with
  no refinement of $o$ in $\Obs(H_2)$.
  Under $P$, the future $H_2$ is realizable and $p$ cannot observe
  the activity in $\bar{S}$ that contributes to it.
  If $p$ exposes $o$, consistency may be violated; if $p$ withholds
  $o$ until the partition heals, availability is violated.
\end{proof}

\noindent
The relationship to Complete CALM:
monotone $\Rightarrow$ distributed-monotone $\Rightarrow$ CAP-free,
and monotone $\Rightarrow$ coordination-free.
The gap between monotonicity and distributed-monotonicity is exactly
the process-local non-monotonicity that coordination can resolve
without communication.
Complete CAP is a semantic characterization: it identifies when no
coordination is required for safety, not how a process algorithmically
selects an outcome.

\paragraph{How coordination is achieved operationally.}
Each non-monotonicity witness $(H_1, H_2, o_1)$ must be resolved by
either excluding $o_1$ from $\Obs'(H_1)$ (\emph{outcome
restriction}) or excluding $H_2$ from the variant's history space
(\emph{future restriction}).
Roughly speaking, future restriction corresponds to pessimistic
schemes and outcome restriction to optimistic schemes.
Table~\ref{tab:mechanisms} classifies well-known mechanisms.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Mechanism} & \textbf{Strategy} & \textbf{Effect} \\
\midrule
Mutual exclusion (locking) & Future restriction & Blocks conflicting futures \\
Total-order broadcast & Future restriction & Serializes all inputs \\
Leader election & Future restriction & Single writer, no conflicts \\
Barriers (MapReduce) & Future restriction & No future until all reach barrier \\
Quorum reads & Outcome restriction & Filters stale values \\

Deferred exposure (shadow copies) & Outcome restriction & Withholds until confirmed safe \\

\end{tabular}

\label{tab:mechanisms}
\end{table}

\begin{example}[Paxos-based log as a properly coordinated variant]
  \label{ex:paxos-log}
  Consider a key-value store replicated at two nodes, with a
  Paxos-based total-order broadcast ensuring all nodes process updates
  in the same sequence.
  The internal specification is linearizability (non-monotone, as
  shown in Section~\ref{sec:example}).
  Paxos restricts admissible histories to those in which all updates
  are totally ordered.

  The exposed output is the prefix of the ordered update log that
  each node has processed.
  Outcomes are log prefixes, ordered by prefix extension.
  For any future $H \hext H'$: the log can only grow, so any prefix
  $o \in \Obs(H)$ remains a prefix of every $o' \in \Obs(H')$.
  The variant is monotone---by Complete CALM, downstream readers need
  no additional coordination.

  Complete CALM makes this boundary explicit: the coordinated
  producer may require coordination, while the exposed log-prefix
  interface is coordination-free for downstream consumers.
  Relational-transducer CALM can see the latter only after the boundary
  has been encoded as a separate monotone input relation.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Stratified Datalog: the canonical separation]
  \label{ex:stratified-separation}
  Consider a 2-stratum Datalog program (e.g., transitive closure in
  stratum~1, unreachability via negation in stratum~2).
  CALM classifies it as non-monotone---coordination is required to seal
  stratum~1 before stratum~2 executes.
  A stratified execution (a barrier between strata) results in a
  specification that is properly coordinated in our sense: after sealing,
  the output of stratum~2 is fixed and grows monotonically under set
  inclusion as the sealed computation completes.
  Complete CALM correctly identifies this resolved output as monotone.
  CALM, applied to the program text, cannot make this
  distinction---it sees negation and reports ``requires coordination''
  regardless of whether the coordination has been provided.
\end{example}

\section{CALM as an Instance}
\label{sec:beyond-calm}
% ================================================================

Complete CALM lifts the CALM theorem from relational transducers to
arbitrary specifications.
We now show that this lifting is faithful: under the natural
instantiation, both criteria accept exactly the monotone queries.

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Formal Subsumption}
\label{sec:calm-instance}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{definition}[Transducer instantiation]
  \label{def:transducer-inst}
  For a query $Q$ over input schema $S_{\mathit{in}}$, define the
  specification $\Spec_Q = (E, \Obs, \subseteq)$ as follows:
  \begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item $E$: input-fact arrivals are external input events.
    \item Histories are input-growth sequences: each history $H$
          determines a set $I_H$ of input facts delivered so far.
    \item $\Obs(H) = \{Q(I_H)\}$---the semantic query result on the
          input facts present at $H$.
    \item $\Ord$ is set inclusion: $o_1 \Ord o_2$ iff
          $o_1 \subseteq o_2$.
  \end{itemize}
  Internal events (rule firings, messages, heartbeats) are abstracted
  away: the specification concerns only the relationship between input
  growth and query output.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Subsumption of CALM]
  \label{thm:subsumption}
  Under the transducer instantiation, the following are equivalent for
  a query $Q$:
  \begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item $Q$ is coordination-free in the sense of Ameloot et
          al.~\cite{ameloot2013relational}: there exists a horizontal
          partition on which quiescence is reached by heartbeat
          transitions alone.
    \item $\Spec_Q$ is monotone in the sense of Complete CALM.
    \item $Q$ is a monotone query (for all $I \subseteq J$,
          $Q(I) \subseteq Q(J)$).
  \end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
  $(3) \Rightarrow (2)$:
  If $Q$ is monotone, then for any $H_1 \hext H_2$,
  $I_{H_1} \subseteq I_{H_2}$.
  By monotonicity of $Q$, $Q(I_{H_1}) \subseteq Q(I_{H_2})$, i.e.,
  $\Obs(H_1) \Ord \Obs(H_2)$.
  Hence $\Spec_Q$ is monotone.

  $(2) \Rightarrow (3)$:
  If $\Spec_Q$ is monotone, consider inputs $I \subseteq J$.
  Let $H_I$ be the history with input facts $I$, and $H_J$ the history
  with input facts $J$.
  Since $I \subseteq J$, $H_I \hext H_J$.
  By monotonicity of $\Spec_Q$:
  $Q(I) = Q(I_{H_I}) \subseteq Q(I_{H_J}) = Q(J)$.
  Hence $Q$ is monotone.

  $(3) \Leftrightarrow (1)$:
  This is the relational-transducer CALM
  theorem~\cite{ameloot2013relational}:
  coordination-free transducers compute exactly the monotone queries
  (Ameloot et al.'s Corollary~13).
\end{proof}

\noindent
The subsumption is exact: under the transducer instantiation, the two
criteria agree.
However, the transducer model's definition of coordination-freedom
bakes in replica consistency---all nodes must eventually produce the
same output set.
This conflation means that specifications which are
coordination-free but not convergent (e.g., causal consistency) cannot
be expressed as coordination-free transducers.
Complete CALM decouples the two properties: monotonicity characterizes
when coordination is avoidable; replica consistency is a separate
structural property of $\Ord$
(Section~\ref{sec:replica-consistency}).

The same argument extends to the full hierarchy of Ameloot, Ketsman,
Neven, and Zinn~\cite{ameloot2015weaker} and to Baccaert and Ketsman's
$\mathbf{C}$-monotonicity~\cite{baccaert2026spectrum}: restricting
$\mathcal{A}$ to histories consistent with each model's information
assumptions yields the corresponding characterization as an instance.
Formal instantiations appear in Appendix~\ref{app:hierarchy}.

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Expressivity Beyond Set Inclusion}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

Complete CALM can diagnose coordination-free specifications that
relational-transducer CALM cannot express.
We use prefix extension as a witness.

\begin{proposition}[Prefix extension requires negation in Datalog]
  \label{prop:prefix-negation}
  Standard encodings of prefix extension as a Datalog program over
  set-inclusion facts require negation (a non-monotone operator).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
  Encoding a sequence as a set of position-value facts
  $\{\mathit{pos}(i, v)\}$ under set inclusion admits forks: two
  values at the same position.
  Recovering prefix semantics (at most one value per position)
  requires a uniqueness constraint, which is non-monotone.
  Full proof in Appendix~\ref{app:hierarchy}.
\end{proof}

\noindent
More generally, any refinement order requiring mutual exclusion
between outcomes (at most one value per position, at most one branch
chosen) needs negation in Datalog---set inclusion 
cannot express such constraints monotonically.

\section{Applications to Data Management}
\label{sec:applications}
% ================================================================

We instantiate Complete CALM on three families of specifications central
to data management.
In each case, the same semantic test---monotonicity---recovers a
known coordination boundary and provides a uniform explanation for why it
arises.

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Transactional Isolation Levels}
\label{sec:isolation}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

Bailis et al.~\cite{bailis2013hat} identify a sharp boundary between
isolation levels achievable with high availability (HATs) and those
requiring coordination.
We recover this boundary as the monotonicity boundary.

\paragraph{Spec sketch.}
Histories include transaction invocations, read/write events, and commit
decisions.
Outcomes are sets of atomic facts
$\{\mathsf{commit}(T), \mathsf{read}(T, x, v), \ldots\}$
recording committed transactions and their observed values.
The outcome order is set inclusion: $o_1 \Ord o_2$ iff $o_1 \subseteq o_2$.
An isolation level $L$ induces $\Spec_L = (E, \Obs_L, \Ord)$ where
$\Obs_L(H)$ contains the outcome fact-sets consistent with $L$'s
correctness constraints.

\begin{proposition}[HAT levels are monotone]
  \label{prop:hat-monotone}
  Under read uncommitted, read committed, and session guarantees
  (monotonic reads, read-your-writes), the specification $\Spec_L$ is
  monotone.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof (read committed; others in Appendix~\ref{app:hierarchy})]
  We show: for all $H \hext H'$ and all $o \in \Obs_L(H)$, there
  exists $o' \in \Obs_L(H')$ with $o \subseteq o'$.
  Under read committed, a read must return a value written by a
  committed transaction.
  Extending a history can add committed transactions but cannot
  uncommit a previously committed one.
  Hence every $\mathsf{read}(T, x, v) \in o$ remains valid at $H'$,
  and $o' = o \cup \{\text{newly committed facts}\} \in \Obs_L(H')$.
  The argument for the other HAT levels is identical in structure:
  each level's constraints are \emph{prefix-stable}---they restrict
  which read values are admissible but never retroactively invalidate
  a previously admissible read.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Serializability is not monotone]
  \label{prop:ser-nonmonotone}
  Under serializability, there exist $H_1 \hext H_2$ and
  $o_1 \in \Obs_{\mathit{ser}}(H_1)$ with no extension in
  $\Obs_{\mathit{ser}}(H_2)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Witness]
  Let $x = y = 0$ initially.
  Transaction $T_1$ reads $x$, writes $y := 1$;
  $T_2$ reads $y$, writes $x := 1$.
  At $H_1$, $T_1$ commits after reading $x = 0$:
  $o_1 = \{\mathsf{commit}(T_1), \mathsf{read}(T_1, x, 0)\}
  \in \Obs_{\mathit{ser}}(H_1)$.
  Extend to $H_2$ where $T_2$ also commits reading $y = 0$.
  Any $o_2 \supseteq o_1$ in $\Obs_{\mathit{ser}}(H_2)$ must include
  both reads, but $\mathsf{read}(T_1, x, 0)$ and
  $\mathsf{read}(T_2, y, 0)$ together induce a cyclic serialization
  order.
  No such $o_2$ exists.
\end{proof}

\noindent
By Complete CALM, serializability intrinsically requires coordination.
This recovers the non-HAT classification
of~\cite{bailis2013hat} as a semantic test.
(Snapshot isolation similarly requires coordination---its
first-committer-wins rule commits to a transaction ordering that
concurrent extensions can invalidate---but the precise witness differs
from serializability's and we omit it here.)

The structural explanation is general: specifications that commit only to
partial-order structure (causal consistency, read committed) are
monotone---a downward-closed cut of happens-before remains valid
under extension.
Specifications that commit to total-order structure across concurrency
(serializability) are not---a linearization commits
to adjacency facts that a concurrent extension can invalidate.
The HAT/non-HAT boundary aligns with the partial-order/total-order
boundary.

\paragraph{Why specification-level analysis matters here.}
Transactional isolation is not naturally expressed as a Datalog program.
One could encode a transaction scheduler in Datalog, but the encoding is
not unique: a scheduler that checks serializability via cycle detection
uses negation (non-monotone), while one that enforces it via two-phase
locking uses only monotone lock acquisition---yet both implement the same
specification.
CALM's diagnosis would depend on which encoding is chosen, not on the
isolation level itself.
Complete CALM sidesteps this entirely: it tests the specification
directly, without any program encoding.

\paragraph{Connection to CAP.}
The preceding analysis is about concurrency: serializability requires
coordination whether the database is distributed or not.
CAP adds the distributional consequence: when the system \emph{is}
distributed, the required coordination may become unavailable under
partitions.
Serializability is not distributed-monotone: a partition-constrained
future (remote transaction commit) can invalidate a local read outcome.
Under partitions, the exposing process cannot observe the remote
commit, forcing a choice between blocking and weakening the isolation
guarantee.
Complete CALM identifies the \emph{source} of this dilemma---non-monotonicity
of the specification---while Complete CAP identifies when that
non-monotonicity creates a CAP-style obstruction (when it manifests
as a failure of distributed-monotonicity).

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Invariant Confluence}
\label{sec:i-confluence}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

Bailis et al.~\cite{bailis2014coordination} show that an application
invariant can be preserved without coordination iff it is
\emph{$I$-confluent}: merging any two invariant-preserving states yields
an invariant-preserving state.
We recover this as an instance of Complete CALM.

\paragraph{Instantiation.}
Model a replicated database where nodes apply transactions locally and
merge via gossip ($E_{\mathit{in}}$: transactions;
$E_{\mathit{send}}/E_{\mathit{recv}}$: gossip;
$E_{\mathit{int}}$: merge events).
The observable at $H$ is the \emph{converged state}
$\mathit{conv}(H)$---the join (merge) of all replica states present
in $H$, i.e., the unique state obtained by merging all updates that
have occurred regardless of delivery order.
Define $\Obs_I(H) = \{\mathit{conv}(H)\}$ when $\mathit{conv}(H)$
satisfies $I$, and $\Obs_I(H) = \emptyset$ otherwise (the history is
unrealizable under any invariant-preserving execution).
The outcome order is the semilattice order induced by merge.

\begin{proposition}[$I$-confluence $\Leftrightarrow$ monotonicity]
  \label{prop:iconfl}
  $\Spec_I$ is monotone iff $T$ is $I$-confluent with respect to $I$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
  $(\Leftarrow)$:
  If $T$ is $I$-confluent and $\mathit{conv}(H_1)$ satisfies $I$,
  then extending $H_1$ to $H_2$ merges additional $I$-valid states
  into the converged state; by $I$-confluence the result satisfies $I$.
  Hence $\Obs_I(H_2) \neq \emptyset$ and the outcome refines.

  $(\Rightarrow)$:
  If $T$ is not $I$-confluent, there exist $I$-valid states $D_1, D_2$
  whose merge violates $I$.
  At $H_1$ (only replica $A$ active, $\mathit{conv}(H_1) = D_1$),
  $\Obs_I(H_1) = \{D_1\}$.
  Extend to $H_2$ by adding $B$'s transaction reaching $D_2$:
  $\mathit{conv}(H_2) = \mathit{merge}(D_1, D_2)$ violates $I$, so
  $\Obs_I(H_2) = \emptyset$.
  The outcome at $H_1$ has no refinement---$\Spec_I$ is not monotone.
\end{proof}

\noindent
By Complete CALM, $I$-confluent invariants admit coordination-free
enforcement; non-$I$-confluent invariants require coordination.
The criterion provides the \emph{why}: non-$I$-confluence is exactly
non-monotonicity of observable state sets under causal extension.

\paragraph{Why CALM is fragile here.}
To apply CALM to invariant enforcement, one must encode the invariant
check as a Datalog program.
A foreign-key constraint (non-monotone: deletion of a referenced tuple
violates it) and a uniqueness constraint (non-monotone: insertion of a
duplicate violates it) would both be classified as ``requires
coordination'' by CALM.
But $I$-confluence distinguishes them: foreign keys are not
$I$-confluent (merging two valid states can violate the constraint),
while uniqueness under certain merge strategies is.
Complete CALM recovers this distinction directly from the specification,
without encoding invariants as Datalog rules.

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{CRDTs and Replicated Objects}
\label{sec:crdts}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

Conflict-free replicated data types~\cite{shapiro2011crdt} achieve
coordination-free replication by restricting updates to inflationary
operations on a join-semilattice.
Complete CALM explains why this works.

\paragraph{Spec sketch.}
A state-based CRDT has state space $(S, \sqcup)$ forming a
join-semilattice.
Updates are inflationary: $\mathit{update}(s) \sqsupseteq s$.
Outcomes are states, ordered by the lattice order:
$o_1 \Ord o_2$ iff $o_1 \sqsubseteq o_2$.
$\Obs(H)$ contains the states reachable by applying updates and merges
consistent with $H$.

\begin{proposition}[CRDTs are monotone]
  \label{prop:crdt-monotone}
  Any specification whose updates are inflationary in a
  join-semilattice and whose outcome order is the lattice order is
  monotone.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  Let $o \in \Obs(H)$ and $H \hext H'$.
  The extension $H'$ may add updates and merges.
  Since updates are inflationary ($\mathit{update}(s) \sqsupseteq s$)
  and merge is join ($s_1 \sqcup s_2 \sqsupseteq s_1$), every state
  reachable at $H'$ is at least as large (in the lattice order) as
  some state reachable at $H$.
  In particular, there exists $o' \in \Obs(H')$ with
  $o \sqsubseteq o'$, i.e., $o \Ord o'$.
\end{proof}

\noindent
CRDTs are monotone \emph{by construction}: the lattice structure
guarantees that observable state can only grow.
Complete CALM certifies that this structural choice is both sufficient
and, in a precise sense, necessary: any coordination-free replicated
object must have monotone observable semantics, and the
semilattice discipline is one way to ensure this.

The converse is instructive.
A replicated counter with a \emph{reset} operation (set to zero) is not
inflationary: after incrementing to $5$, a reset produces a state
incomparable to $5$ under any natural order.
If the outcome order treats $0$ and $5$ as contradictory, the
specification is not monotone and coordination is required to
implement reset consistently.
This matches the known result that reset-capable CRDTs require
additional protocol machinery~\cite{shapiro2011crdt}.

\paragraph{CALM and CRDTs.}
CRDTs were designed independently of CALM, using lattice theory rather
than Datalog monotonicity.
CALM can analyze a CRDT only if its merge and update logic is first
encoded as a Datalog program---an unnatural exercise that obscures the
lattice structure.
Complete CALM applies directly to the CRDT's specification (states
ordered by the lattice order), recognizing monotonicity as a
consequence of the semilattice structure without any encoding step.

\medskip
\noindent
Across all three application families, the same phenomenon recurs:
coordination is required exactly when observable outcomes commit to
structure that a future extension can invalidate.
For isolation levels, the fragile structure is a global serialization
order; for invariant confluence, it is a reachable-state set closed
under merge; for CRDTs, it is the lattice order itself.
Monotonicity is the uniform diagnostic that identifies this
boundary in each case.


%----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Coordination-Freedom vs.\ Replica Consistency}
\label{sec:replica-consistency}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

A natural expectation is that coordination-freedom and replica
consistency are the same concern.
Mahajan et al.~\cite{mahajan2011consistency} established that they are
independent: plain causal consistency~\cite{ahamad1995causal} is
coordination-free but not convergent (replicas may permanently
disagree on the ordering of concurrent operations).
CRDTs~\cite{shapiro2011crdt} achieve both via join-semilattice
structure; linearizability achieves convergence but requires
coordination.

In our framework, the distinction reduces to a structural property of
$\Ord$: if $\Ord$ admits joins and the implementation merges
divergent outcomes by join, monotonicity implies convergence
(divergent replicas can always reconcile); if $\Ord$ lacks joins,
monotonicity guarantees safe independent action but not convergence.
A reader whose primary concern is replica consistency can use Complete
CALM as a two-part test: (1)~is the specification monotone?
(2)~does $\Ord$ admit joins?
If both hold, the specification admits coordination-free convergent
implementation---recovering the CRDT guarantee as a special case.
Section~\ref{sec:crdts} develops this connection formally.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ================================================================

\paragraph{CALM and relational transducers.}
The CALM theorem, conjectured by Hellerstein~\cite{hellerstein2010declarative}
and proved by Ameloot et al.~\cite{ameloot2013relational}, establishes that
monotone queries admit coordination-free distributed evaluation in the
relational transducer model.
Complete CALM subsumes this: relational transducers with
set-inclusion order on output facts are one instantiation of our framework
(Section~\ref{sec:calm-instance}).
The key advance is that our criterion operates on \emph{observable output
semantics} rather than program syntax, enabling verification that
coordination correctly discharges a non-monotone specification---a
capability absent from the transducer-based analysis.
Ameloot et al.~\cite{ameloot2015weaker} and Baccaert and
Ketsman~\cite{baccaert2026spectrum} generalize CALM by varying what
information nodes possess (an epistemic dimension orthogonal to ours);
Appendix~\ref{app:hierarchy} shows that our lifting accommodates their
contributions as instances.
Li and Lee~\cite{li2025coordinationfree} develop a CALM-style
equivalence for replicated objects; their model assumes replication
and merge as primitives, whereas ours treats replication as one
instance of causal history growth.

\paragraph{Free termination.}
Power et al.~\cite{power2025freetermination} study a complementary
question: not whether a node can safely \emph{emit} without
coordination (safety, our concern), but whether it can
\emph{terminate}---know its output is final---without coordination.
Their free termination property is strictly stronger than monotonicity:
a grow-only set CRDT is monotone but cannot detect quiescence.
The two results are orthogonal but share structural DNA: both show that
algebraic properties of the outcome/state space (partial orders,
acyclicity) determine what can be achieved without coordination.

\paragraph{HATs and I-confluence.}
Highly Available Transactions~\cite{bailis2013hat} identify isolation levels
achievable without coordination; I-confluence~\cite{bailis2014coordination}
characterizes invariants preservable without coordination.
Both are instances of Complete CALM (Section~\ref{sec:applications}).

\paragraph{CRDTs and eventual consistency.}
Shapiro et al.~\cite{shapiro2011crdt} ensure coordination-free replication
by restricting updates to inflationary operations on join-semilattices.
In our terms, CRDTs inhabit a well-behaved subspace of monotone
specifications (Section~\ref{sec:crdts}).
Burckhardt~\cite{burckhardt2014principles} provides a comprehensive
framework for eventual consistency; our criterion provides the sharp
boundary between specifications that admit it and those that do not.

\paragraph{CAP and partition tolerance.}
Brewer's conjecture~\cite{brewer2000towards} and the Gilbert--Lynch
proof~\cite{gilbert2002cap} show that linearizability cannot coexist
with availability and partition tolerance---a one-directional
impossibility for a single consistency model.
Complete CAP (Section~\ref{sec:complete-cap}) generalizes this to a
bidirectional characterization: a specification admits a consistent,
available, partition-tolerant implementation iff it is
distributed-monotone.
The generalization factors CAP into a semantic condition
(distributed-monotonicity: outcomes survive remote-only futures) and
an operational observation (partitions produce exactly such futures),
composing them into the full iff.

\paragraph{Arbitration-free consistency.}
Attiya et al.~\cite{attiya2023arbitration} prove that a storage
specification admits an available implementation iff it is
\emph{arbitration-free} (requires no total order).
Their criterion is a special case of ours: total-order arbitration is
one form of non-monotonicity. Our framework additionally handles
non-total-order coordination (quorum reads, locking) and supports 
general verification of proper coordination: even subtle interleavings of
coordination into code can be checked.

\paragraph{Language-level monotonicity.}
LVars~\cite{kuper2013lvars} introduced
language-level support for lattice-based monotonic growth, guaranteeing
determinism via threshold reads.
The Lasp language~\cite{meiklejohn2015lasp} extends this to
distributed CRDTs via lattice types.
The type system of Gallifrey~\cite{milano2019tour} exposes monotonicity
over multiple different orders, with coordination at the transitions
between them.
These designs influenced our decision to make the outcome order
$\Ord$ a first-class component of a specification.
The Hydro distributed programming framework~\cite{hydro} uses
type-level monotonicity annotations to conservatively verify
coordination-freedom at compile time---an instance of the
sufficient-condition approach to the undecidable general problem.


% ================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ================================================================

Complete CALM provides a universal semantic criterion for
coordination-freedom: a specification admits a coordination-free
implementation if and only if its observable outcomes are
monotone.
The criterion operates on specifications directly---independent of
programming language, computational model, or protocol structure---and
subsumes relational-transducer CALM as a formal instance.

The separation from CALM is not merely theoretical.
It captures a pattern that pervades modern data infrastructure:
coordination layers (consensus protocols, two-phase commit, serializable
schedulers) resolve non-monotonicity internally, producing monotone
output streams that downstream systems consume without coordination.
Complete CALM provides the first formal basis for reasoning about this
separation---verifying that coordination \emph{correctly discharges} a
non-monotone specification, rather than merely detecting that
non-monotonicity exists.

The applications demonstrate that known coordination boundaries---HAT
isolation levels, $I$-confluence, CRDT semilattice
discipline---are instances of a single semantic test.
Monotonicity is the common structural property; its failure is
the universal explanation for why coordination is required.
In the distributed setting, Complete CAP refines this:
specifications that fail distributed-monotonicity---those whose
outcomes can be invalidated by remote-only futures---cannot be
implemented with consistency, availability, and partition tolerance
simultaneously.

\paragraph{Beyond CALM.}
The methodology demonstrated here---identify the minimal semantic
ingredients of a model-specific result, prove the characterization at
that level, and recover the original as an instance---may apply to
other results in database theory that appear to depend on the
relational model.
Stratification theory (when is negation safe?) may lift to a semantic
characterization of sequential commitment layers.
Datalog boundedness (does a program terminate in bounded iterations?)
may lift to finite determination depth over specifications.
Query containment may lift to subsumption of coordination-free
frontiers.
In each case, the question is: what does the proof actually need, and
what is model-specific scaffolding?
Complete CALM suggests that the answer is often less than expected.

\section{Complete CAP: Formal Treatment}
\label{app:proofs}
\label{app:cap-formal}

We formalize CAP within our framework using the partition-constrained
futures from Section~\ref{sec:complete-cap}.
The appendix provides the availability definition and a detailed proof.

\begin{definition}[Maximal availability under partitions]
  \label{def:maximal-availability}
  Fix $\Spec = (E, \Obs, \Ord)$ and a partition $P = (S, \bar{S})$.
  Let $\mathsf{Ext}_P(H_i)$ be the $P$-constrained extensions of
  $H_i$ (futures with no cross-partition message delivery).
  An implementation $I$ is \emph{maximally available under $P$}
  if whenever some $H \in \mathsf{Ext}_P(H_i)$ both completes a
  client invocation $e$ and admits a nonempty $\Obs(H)$, there exists
  an execution $H^* \in \mathsf{Ext}_P(H_i)$ of $I$ in which $e$
  completes.
\end{definition}

\noindent
This is a semantic availability condition: if a valid response is
possible under the partition, the implementation must have an execution
that produces one.
Stronger universal/fairness-based availability notions (``every
request to a non-failing node eventually receives a response'') only
strengthen the impossibility direction of the theorem.

\begin{theorem}[Complete CAP, restated]
  \label{cor:cap-formal}
  Let $\Spec = (E, \Obs, \Ord)$ be a specification with process
  assignment $\pi$ in the asynchronous message-passing model.
  $\Spec$ admits an implementation that is both correct on all
  well-formed histories and maximally available under all
  partitions if and only if $\Spec$ is distributed-monotone.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
  ($\Rightarrow$, not distributed-monotone implies CAP obstruction.)
  If $\Spec$ is not distributed-monotone, there exists a partition
  $P = (S, \bar{S})$, a process $p \in S$, an outcome
  $o \in \Obs(H_1)$ at $p$, and a $P$-constrained future $H_2$ with
  no refinement of $o$ in $\Obs(H_2)$.

  Construct two executions indistinguishable to $p$:
  \begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \emph{Execution $\alpha$}: the system is at $H_1$; no
          further activity occurs in $\bar{S}$ (the partition holds
          and $\bar{S}$ is idle).
          Process $p$'s local view is its projection of $H_1$.
    \item \emph{Execution $\beta$}: the system is at $H_1$;
          processes in $\bar{S}$ advance to produce $H_2$ (a
          $P$-constrained future---no cross-partition delivery).
          Process $p$'s local view is identical to $\alpha$ (it
          receives no messages from $\bar{S}$ in either case).
  \end{itemize}
  In both executions, $p$ sees the same local state at the decision
  point.
  If $p$ exposes $o$ (as required by availability in $\alpha$, where
  $o$ is valid), then in $\beta$ the system reaches $H_2$ where no
  outcome refines $o$---correctness is violated.
  If $p$ withholds $o$ to protect against $\beta$, it blocks
  indefinitely in $\alpha$ (the partition persists, $\bar{S}$ is
  idle, and $p$ never learns which execution it is
  in)---availability is violated.

  Hence at least one of correctness or availability must be
  sacrificed.

  ($\Leftarrow$, distributed-monotone implies no CAP obstruction.)
  If $\Spec$ is distributed-monotone, then for every partition $P$,
  every process $p \in S$, and every $P$-constrained future $H_2$ of
  $H_1$, every outcome $o \in \Obs(H_1)$ at $p$ has a refinement in
  $\Obs(H_2)$.
  Under any partition, the only realizable futures are
  $P$-constrained.
  Since every outcome survives all such futures, $p$ can expose any
  admissible outcome immediately---both correctness and availability
  hold regardless of partition state.
\end{proof}

\noindent
CAP is not tied to linearizability.
It applies to any specification that fails distributed-monotonicity:
one whose outcomes can be invalidated by a partition-constrained
future.
For distributed-monotone specifications, no partition-constrained
future can invalidate any outcome, so CAP is vacuous.

\section{The Coordination-Free Frontier}
\label{app:minimality}

For a given system (fixed $E$ and $\Obs$), different choices of
outcome order $\Ord$ yield different coordination requirements.
A natural question: given a non-monotone specification
$(E, \Obs, \Ord)$, what are the strongest guarantees achievable
without coordination?

Since enlarging $\Ord$ (adding edges) can only help monotonicity
(more outcomes qualify as refinements), the coordination-free
alternatives are the \emph{minimal monotone enlargements} of $\Ord$:
orders $\Ord' \supseteq \Ord$ that are monotone, with no proper
subset of $\Ord'$ that contains $\Ord$ and is also monotone.
These form a \emph{frontier}---a set of incomparable alternatives,
each representing a different coordination-free weakening of the
original guarantee.

\begin{definition}[Coordination-free frontier]
  \label{def:frontier}
  For a specification $(E, \Obs, \Ord)$, the \emph{coordination-free
  frontier} is the set of minimal (by edge-set inclusion) partial
  orders $\Ord' \supseteq \Ord$ such that $(E, \Obs, \Ord')$ is
  monotone.
\end{definition}

\noindent
More generally, a coordination-free weakening may change the
observation function, the refinement order, or both---yielding a
weaker \emph{interface} rather than merely a relaxed order.
The register result below identifies causal consistency as the
strongest coordination-free interface below linearizability
under this broader comparison.

\noindent
Each point on the frontier is a coordination-free weakening of the
original spec: it preserves all the original refinement relationships
and adds just enough new ones to achieve monotonicity.
The gap between $\Ord$ and a frontier element $\Ord'$ represents
transitions the client considers incompatible but that the system
cannot prevent without coordination.

We illustrate with three worked instances under their stated
assumptions, showing that well-known coordination-free consistency
models lie on the frontier of their stronger counterparts.

\subsection{Register: Causal Consistency}

\paragraph{Specification.}
Fix a replicated register at nodes $p_1, \ldots, p_n$.
Events: write invocations $w(v)$ and read invocations $r$ at each
node (in $E_{\mathit{in}}$); propagation messages (in
$E_{\mathit{send}}, E_{\mathit{recv}}$).
A history $H$ determines, for each read $r$, the set of writes in
$r$'s causal past: $\mathit{past}(r, H) = \{w \mid w \rightarrow^* r
\text{ in } H\}$.

Under \emph{linearizability}, outcomes are sequences of
committed operations ordered by prefix extension ($\Ord_{\mathit{lin}}$
= prefix).
Under \emph{causal consistency}, define:
\[
  \Obs_{\mathit{causal}}(H) = \bigl\{\, o \mid
  \text{every read } r \text{ in } o \text{ returns some }
  v \text{ with } w(v) \in \mathit{past}(r, H) \text{ or }
  v = v_0 \text{ (initial value) and no write is in }
  \mathit{past}(r, H) \,\bigr\}.
\]
The outcome order is prefix extension:
$o_1 \Ord_{\mathit{causal}} o_2$ iff $o_1$ is a prefix of $o_2$.

\begin{proposition}[Register interface frontier]
  \label{prop:register-frontier}
  Causal consistency is a monotone weakening of linearizability
  for the replicated register: it preserves read/write histories while
  weakening total-order outcomes to causal-prefix outcomes.
  Under this interface comparison, causal consistency is on the
  coordination-free frontier.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  \emph{Monotonicity.}
  Let $H_1 \hext H_2$ and $o_1 \in \Obs_{\mathit{causal}}(H_1)$.
  Every read in $o_1$ returns a value written in its causal past at
  $H_1$.
  By Definition~\ref{def:future}(iii), $H_1$ is downward-closed in
  $H_2$: no new event in $H_2$ enters the causal past of any event
  in $H_1$.
  Hence $\mathit{past}(r, H_2) \supseteq \mathit{past}(r, H_1)$ for
  every read $r$ already in $H_1$, and the return value chosen in
  $o_1$ remains valid.
  New reads in $H_2$ can be appended with any causally-consistent
  return value.
  Thus $o_1$ extends to some $o_2 \in \Obs_{\mathit{causal}}(H_2)$
  with $o_1 \Ord o_2$.

  \emph{Maximality.}
  Let $\Ord'$ be any order smaller than $\Ord_{\mathit{causal}}$
  (i.e., $\Ord' \subsetneq \Ord_{\mathit{causal}}$).
  Then there exist outcomes $o_1, o_2$ with
  $o_1 \Ord_{\mathit{causal}} o_2$ but $o_1 \not\Ord' o_2$.
  Since $o_1 \Ord_{\mathit{causal}} o_2$, outcome $o_2$ extends $o_1$
  by appending at least one operation---call it $r \mapsto v$ (a read
  returning $v$).
  We construct $H_1, H_2$ witnessing that the removal of this
  refinement edge breaks monotonicity.

  \emph{Construction.}
  Let $H_1$ contain: a single write $w(v)$ at node $p$, delivered to
  node $q$ ($\mathit{recv}(w(v))$ at $q$), and all operations in
  $o_1$ completed.
  The outcome $o_1 \in \Obs_{\mathit{causal}}(H_1)$ by assumption.
  Extend to $H_2$ by adding a read invocation $r$ at $q$ with
  $\mathit{recv}(w(v)) \rightarrow \mathit{inv}(r)$ (the read is
  causally after the delivery of $w(v)$), and no other write in $r$'s
  causal past writes a different value.
  Then $\mathit{past}(r, H_2) = \{w(v)\}$: the only write in $r$'s
  causal past is $w(v)$, so the unique causally-consistent return
  value is $v$.
  Hence the only outcome in $\Obs_{\mathit{causal}}(H_2)$ extending
  $o_1$ is $o_2 = o_1 \cdot (r \mapsto v)$.
  Since $o_1 \not\Ord' o_2$, the outcome $o_1$ has no
  $\Ord'$-refinement at $H_2$: $(E, \Obs, \Ord')$ is not monotone.
  Hence no order smaller than $\Ord_{\mathit{causal}}$ is monotone.
\end{proof}

\noindent
This recovers the result of Mahajan et
al.~\cite{mahajan2011consistency}: causal consistency is the strongest
coordination-free consistency model for read/write registers.
Our derivation is purely semantic (from the frontier construction)
rather than operational (from a specific protocol).

\subsection{Queue: Causal FIFO}

\paragraph{Specification.}
Fix a replicated FIFO queue at nodes $p_1, \ldots, p_n$ with
enqueue($v$) and dequeue operations.
Events: enqueue and dequeue invocations at each node (in
$E_{\mathit{in}}$); propagation messages (in
$E_{\mathit{send}}, E_{\mathit{recv}}$).
A history $H$ determines a set of enqueued values and a causal order
among enqueue events.

Under \emph{strict FIFO}, outcomes are dequeue sequences consistent
with a single global total order on enqueues:
\[
  \Obs_{\mathit{FIFO}}(H) = \bigl\{\, \sigma \mid
  \sigma \text{ is a prefix of some total order on enqueued values
  consistent with } H\text{'s causal order} \,\bigr\}.
\]
Under \emph{causal FIFO}, the total-order requirement is relaxed to
causal order only:
\[
  \Obs_{\mathit{causal\text{-}FIFO}}(H) = \bigl\{\, \sigma \mid
  \text{for all } a, b \text{ in } \sigma\text{: if }
  \mathit{enq}(a) \rightarrow^* \mathit{enq}(b) \text{ in } H
  \text{ then } a \text{ precedes } b \text{ in } \sigma
  \,\bigr\}.
\]
Both use prefix extension as the outcome order.

\begin{proposition}[Queue frontier]
  \label{prop:queue-frontier}
  Causal FIFO is a natural monotone weakening of the replicated FIFO
  queue: it preserves causal ordering among enqueues while relaxing
  the global total-order requirement.
  Under the stated assumptions, causal FIFO is on the
  coordination-free frontier.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  \emph{Monotonicity.}
  Let $H_1 \hext H_2$ and
  $\sigma \in \Obs_{\mathit{causal\text{-}FIFO}}(H_1)$.
  The sequence $\sigma$ respects causal order among enqueues at $H_1$.
  By Definition~\ref{def:future}(iii), extending to $H_2$ adds only
  causally-later events: any new enqueue $c$ in $H_2 \setminus H_1$
  is causally after (or concurrent with) all enqueues in $H_1$.
  In particular, $c$ is not causally \emph{before} any element already
  in $\sigma$.
  Hence $\sigma$ remains a valid causal-FIFO prefix at $H_2$, and can
  be extended by appending $c$ at any position after elements that
  causally precede it.
  Thus $\sigma$ extends to some
  $\sigma' \in \Obs_{\mathit{causal\text{-}FIFO}}(H_2)$.

  \emph{Maximality.}
  Let $\Ord'$ be any order smaller than $\Ord_{\mathit{causal-FIFO}}$
  (i.e., $\Ord' \subsetneq \Ord_{\mathit{causal-FIFO}}$).
  Then there exist sequences $\sigma_1, \sigma_2$ with
  $\sigma_1 \Ord_{\mathit{causal-FIFO}} \sigma_2$ (i.e., $\sigma_1$
  is a prefix of $\sigma_2$) but
  $\sigma_1 \not\Ord' \sigma_2$.
  Since $\sigma_2$ extends $\sigma_1$ by appending some value $b$,
  we have $\sigma_1 \not\Ord' \sigma_1 \cdot b$: the order $\Ord'$
  does not consider $\sigma_1 \cdot b$ a valid refinement of
  $\sigma_1$.

  We construct a witness.
  Let $a$ be the last element of $\sigma_1$ and let $a, b$ be
  enqueued concurrently (neither causally precedes the other).
  Construct $H_1$ containing both enqueues with $a$ dequeued:
  $\sigma_1 \in \Obs_{\mathit{causal-FIFO}}(H_1)$.
  Extend to $H_2$ by adding a dequeue of $b$ (causally after the
  dequeue of $a$):
  $\sigma_1 \cdot b \in \Obs_{\mathit{causal-FIFO}}(H_2)$.
  Since $a$ and $b$ are concurrent, causal FIFO admits both
  $\langle \ldots, a, b \rangle$ and $\langle \ldots, b, a \rangle$
  as valid dequeue orders.
  But at $H_2$, the only extension of $\sigma_1$ is
  $\sigma_1 \cdot b$ (the dequeue event for $b$ is the only new
  completed dequeue in $H_2$).
  Since $\sigma_1 \not\Ord' \sigma_1 \cdot b$, the outcome
  $\sigma_1$ has no $\Ord'$-refinement at $H_2$:
  $(E, \Obs, \Ord')$ is not monotone.
  Hence no order smaller than causal FIFO is monotone.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Search Structures: Forward-Reachability}

\paragraph{Specification.}
Fix a search structure (tree, skip list, hash directory) supporting
insert($k$) and lookup($k$) operations over a key space $K$.
Events: insert and lookup invocations (in $E_{\mathit{in}}$);
structural modifications are internal events (in $E_{\mathit{int}}$).
A history $H$ determines a \emph{structure graph} $G(H) = (V, E)$:
nodes are positions in the structure, edges are navigable links.
Each position $p \in V$ holds a set of keys $\mathit{keys}(p, H)$.

Under \emph{exact-location} semantics:
$\Obs_{\mathit{exact}}(H) = \{(k, p) \mid k \in \mathit{keys}(p, H)\}$
---a lookup for $k$ must arrive at the position currently holding $k$.
Under \emph{forward-reachability} semantics:
\[
  \Obs_{\mathit{fwd}}(H) = \bigl\{\, (k, p) \mid
  \exists \text{ path } p \to p_1 \to \cdots \to p_m \text{ in }
  G(H) \text{ with } k \in \mathit{keys}(p_m, H) \,\bigr\}.
\]
A lookup arriving at $p$ is valid if $k$ is reachable from $p$ in the
structure graph.
The outcome order is set inclusion on valid lookup pairs.

\begin{proposition}[Search frontier]
  \label{prop:search-frontier}
  If structural modifications in the search structure satisfy the
  \emph{link invariant}---whenever a key $k$ moves from position $p$
  to position $p'$, a directed edge from $p$ toward $p'$ exists in
  $G(H)$ after the modification---then forward-reachability is
  a natural monotone weakening for lookup, and under the link
  invariant no weaker order suffices.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  \emph{Monotonicity.}
  Let $H_1 \hext H_2$ and $(k, p) \in \Obs_{\mathit{fwd}}(H_1)$:
  $k$ is reachable from $p$ in $G(H_1)$.
  A structural modification in $H_2$ may move $k$ from its current
  position $p_m$ to a new position $p'$.
  By the link invariant, an edge $p_m \to p'$ exists in $G(H_2)$.
  Hence the path $p \to \cdots \to p_m \to p'$ witnesses
  $(k, p) \in \Obs_{\mathit{fwd}}(H_2)$.
  The outcome $(k, p)$ persists: forward-reachability is monotone.

  \emph{Maximality.}
  Let $\Ord'$ be any order smaller than set inclusion on
  $\Obs_{\mathit{fwd}}$ (i.e., $\Ord' \subsetneq \subseteq$).
  Then there exists a pair $(k, p)$ with
  $(k, p) \in \Obs_{\mathit{fwd}}(H)$ for some $H$ but
  $(k, p) \notin \Ord'$-refinements of some outcome containing it.
  Concretely: there exist outcomes $o_1 \subseteq o_2$ with
  $(k, p) \in o_2 \setminus o_1$, $o_1 \subseteq o_2$ under set
  inclusion, but $o_1 \not\Ord' o_2$.

  \emph{Construction.}
  Let $H_1$ be a history where $k$ is at position $p_m$ reachable
  from $p$ via a path $p \to \cdots \to p_m$, and the outcome
  $o_1 \in \Obs_{\mathit{fwd}}(H_1)$ does not yet include $(k, p)$
  (e.g., no lookup for $k$ starting at $p$ has completed).
  Extend to $H_2$ by adding a lookup for $k$ starting at $p$: since
  $k$ is reachable from $p$, the pair $(k, p)$ enters
  $\Obs_{\mathit{fwd}}(H_2)$, giving $o_2 = o_1 \cup \{(k, p)\}$.
  Now extend further to $H_3$ by a structural modification that moves
  $k$ from $p_m$ to a new position $p'$ (with the link invariant
  providing edge $p_m \to p'$).
  Under forward-reachability, $(k, p)$ remains valid at $H_3$ (the
  path extends: $p \to \cdots \to p_m \to p'$).
  Under exact-location, $(k, p)$ would be invalid at $H_3$ ($k$ is no
  longer at $p_m$, and $p \neq p'$).
  Since $\Ord'$ excludes the refinement $o_1 \Ord' o_2$, the outcome
  $o_1$ at $H_1$ has no $\Ord'$-refinement at $H_2$---monotonicity
  fails.
  Hence no order smaller than forward-reachability (under the link
  invariant) is monotone.
\end{proof}

\noindent
The link invariant is the precise structural condition that makes
forward-reachability achievable.
The Lehman--Yao B-link tree~\cite{lehman1981efficient} satisfies it:
splits install right-pointers before moving keys.
Skip lists, concurrent hash tables with chaining, and other structures
that maintain forwarding links under restructuring likewise satisfy it.
The frontier characterizes \emph{what} coordination-free lookup
requires (forward-reachability under the link invariant); the
implementation determines \emph{how} the invariant is maintained.

\noindent
The three frontier elements exhibit a common pattern: each resolves
non-monotonicity by tolerating \emph{locally valid but globally
imprecise} observations.
For registers, a stale read is superseded by eventual delivery.
For queues, a locally-valid dequeue order among concurrent enqueues
remains valid---concurrent elements may be ordered differently
elsewhere without contradiction.
For search structures, arrival at a stale position is corrected by
following a navigable path to the current location.
In each case, the frontier element is the minimal monotone order that makes
imprecision detectable and recoverable without coordination.

When the specification is defined over a growing graph with
set-inclusion order on outcomes (e.g., reachability facts accumulate as
edges are added), the frontier is the specification itself---it is
already monotone.
This recovers relational-transducer CALM as the graph-structure
instance of the frontier construction.

\paragraph{Classical concurrent specifications.}
The frontier framework also covers classical PODC results as instances.
Global snapshots (downward-closed cuts of happens-before) are monotone
under set inclusion---they are already at the frontier.
Atomic snapshots (committing to a linearization point) are not---they
are registers under sequential-consistency order.
$k$-set agreement and strong renaming are non-monotone for structural
reasons (bounding decision diversity requires total-order commitment).
Consensus is simply a write-once register under equality order---the
frontier is trivial (no weakening preserves the single-value
commitment).
In each case, the frontier construction gives the same answer as
bespoke impossibility proofs, but via a single uniform technique.

\section{Universal Construction}
\label{app:universal}

This appendix shows that for any specification, an ordering authority
(membership establishment plus consistent ordered delivery) yields a
monotone residual specification.
The ordering authority is itself ongoing coordination, but it is
generic and specification-independent; downstream consumption of the
ordered stream is coordination-free.

\begin{remark}[Interface residual vs.\ restriction variant]
  The universal construction produces a \emph{new} output interface
  (ordered log prefixes under prefix extension), not a restriction of
  the original specification's outcome domain.
  This is an \emph{interface residual}---distinct from the
  ``properly coordinated variant'' of
  Definition~\ref{def:coord-variant}, which restricts outcomes within
  the same domain.
  Both achieve monotonicity; they differ in whether the client-facing
  interface changes.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Universal Residualization via an Ordering Authority}
\label{sec:universal-construction}
%----------------------------------------------------------------

The separation theorem exhibits specifications where coordination
resolves non-monotonicity and produces monotone output.
We now show this is always possible: for \emph{any} specification,
establishing a membership authority and an ordering service yields a
monotone residual specification.
The ordering service is itself an ongoing coordination mechanism
(e.g., total-order broadcast, a consensus-based sequencer); the
contribution is that it isolates \emph{all} coordination into a single
reusable layer, after which downstream consumption is
coordination-free.

The key insight is that membership knowledge converts universal
quantification into existential quantification.
A non-monotone operation like ``all participants have contributed''
requires knowing who the participants \emph{are} before it can be
evaluated.
Once membership is established, the check becomes monotone: the set of
received contributions only grows, and once it equals the known
membership, the condition fires.
This is the same mechanism underlying the Immerman--Vardi
theorem~\cite{immerman1986relational}: bounded quantification over a
known domain is expressible in fixpoint logic.

\begin{theorem}[Universal sufficiency of ordering authority]
  \label{thm:universal}
  For any specification $\Spec = (E, \Obs, \Ord)$, there exists a
  coordination mechanism $I_{\mathit{ord}}$ consisting of
  (a)~a membership authority (making the participant set common
  knowledge) and (b)~an ordering service (delivering a consistent
  total extension of the causal order to all replicas) such that the
  resolved specification $\Spec|_{I_{\mathit{ord}}}$ is monotone.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
  \emph{Construction.}
  The mechanism has two components.
  First, a membership authority publishes the set of participants
  $\mathsf{All}$ (a one-time coordination act).
  Second, an ordering service places all input events into a total
  order consistent with causality and delivers this order identically
  to every replica.
  The ordering service operates \emph{online}: events are ordered and
  delivered incrementally as they arrive, not batched.
  Any protocol that produces a consistent total extension of the causal
  partial order suffices (e.g., total-order broadcast, replicated state
  machines, or any consensus-based sequencer).
  The ordering service is itself ongoing coordination---it requires
  consensus per batch or entry---but it is a generic, reusable layer
  independent of the specification.

  Different runs may produce different total orders---the choice among
  valid extensions is nondeterministic---but within a run, all replicas
  observe the same sequence.
  This is the standard semantics of replicated state machines:
  consistency is across replicas (within a run), not across runs.
  The specification's admissible set captures exactly this
  nondeterminism---multiple outcomes are valid, and the ordering
  mechanism selects one per run.

  Each replica evaluates the specification deterministically over the
  growing prefix of ordered events.
  Define the resolved specification's outcomes as these prefix-indexed
  evaluation results, ordered by prefix extension:
  $o_1 \Ord o_2$ iff $o_1$ corresponds to a prefix of the sequence
  that $o_2$ corresponds to.
  Note that this defines a \emph{new} residual output interface
  (ordered-prefix results), not the original specification's outcome
  domain.
  The construction does not preserve arbitrary original observation
  interfaces; it constructs a monotone interface exposing the
  deterministic evaluation of each log prefix.

  \emph{Monotonicity.}
  The ordered sequence only grows (events are appended, never
  reordered or retracted).
  Hence the evaluation result at any prefix refines monotonically:
  for $H_1 \hext H_2$, the prefix at $H_2$ extends the prefix at
  $H_1$, so $o_1 \Ord o_2$.
  The resolved specification is monotone.

  \emph{Separation of concerns.}
  The ordering service is the coordination mechanism; it runs
  continuously and requires distributed agreement.
  But downstream of the ordering service, each replica simply
  accumulates an ever-growing prefix of the agreed sequence.
  No replica needs to suppress any admissible future---it simply
  processes events as they arrive in the agreed order.
  By Complete CALM, this consumption phase is coordination-free.
  The construction thus isolates all coordination into the ordering
  layer; no specification-specific coordination is needed beyond it.
\end{proof}

\noindent
The construction does not claim that the ordering service is
coordination-free---it is not.
Rather, it shows that coordination can always be \emph{factored}:
a generic ordering authority (independent of the specification)
plus coordination-free local evaluation (dependent on the
specification but requiring no further distributed agreement).
Once participants are known and inputs are ordered, all remaining
computation is monotone local accumulation.

\paragraph{Comparison with Ameloot et al.}
Ameloot et al.~\cite{ameloot2013relational} prove a related result in
the transducer model: if nodes have access to the system relations
$\mathsf{All}$ (the set of all node identifiers) and $\mathsf{Id}$
(the local identifier)---the \emph{non-oblivious} setting---then
monotone queries can be evaluated coordination-free.
Our construction improves on this in two ways.

First, it requires no syntactic recognition of special relations.
Ameloot's proof depends on the program having access to $\mathsf{All}$
and $\mathsf{Id}$ and on the analysis recognizing these as system
relations with specific semantics.
Our construction is purely semantic: it operates on the specification
$\Spec$ without inspecting any program text.
Membership is a generic coordination mechanism, not a model-specific
assumption about available relations.

Second, it accommodates membership change at the mechanism level.
Ameloot's non-obliviousness assumes a fixed, globally known set of
nodes---the $\mathsf{All}$ relation is static.
If membership changes (nodes join or depart), the preconditions of the
theorem fail and must be re-established.
Our construction has the same formal requirement (a fixed event universe
$E$), but the mechanism that establishes membership can be extended
incrementally: the current authority uses its decision-making power to
admit new members or appoint successors.
This appointment is itself a monotone extension of the authority
chain---each membership-change event extends the history without
retracting prior membership decisions.
The chain of authorization is constructed incrementally from an original
seed authority (typically defined offline in a configuration file at
system setup).
Downstream coordination-freedom is a property of the \emph{output
semantics} (monotone accumulation over known membership), not of any
static membership assumption.

\paragraph{Architectural consequence.}
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal} formally justifies a common systems
pattern: establish membership once (via a configuration service,
bootstrap protocol, or seed authority), then process all subsequent
computation coordination-free.
Log-based architectures (Kafka~\cite{kreps2011kafka}, event sourcing,
change data capture) are one realization: the log's sequencer is
membership authority, and downstream consumers accumulate log entries
monotonically.
But the theorem is more general---any architecture that establishes
membership and then accumulates inputs monotonically achieves
coordination-freedom downstream, regardless of the specification's
non-monotone structure.

\begin{remark}[Distributed vs.\ local coordination]
  \label{rem:local-depth}
  The universal construction isolates all \emph{distributed}
  coordination into two components: a one-time membership
  establishment and an ongoing ordering service.
  Once membership is known and inputs are consistently ordered,
  downstream evaluation is coordination-free.
  However, local evaluation may still require sequential commitment
  that does not involve distributed coordination.

  Consider a multi-stratum Datalog program evaluated across $n$ nodes.
  Each stratum must be \emph{sealed} before the next can begin: a node
  must know that all derivations in stratum $k$ are complete before
  evaluating negation in stratum $k{+}1$.
  This sealing requires waiting for end-of-data signals from all
  participants---but that waiting is \emph{monotone}: the set of
  received signals only grows, and once all have arrived, the seal
  commits locally.
  No distributed coordination is needed beyond knowing who the
  participants are (membership).

  The distributed coordination depth for stratified evaluation is
  therefore one act: establishing membership.
  After that, each stratum seal is a local commitment triggered by
  monotone accumulation of signals from known participants.
  The $k$ strata require $k$ local sequential commitments, but zero
  additional rounds of distributed coordination.
  (The ordering service itself requires ongoing consensus, but that is
  a generic reusable layer independent of the stratified program.)
\end{remark}


\section{Subsumption of the CALM Hierarchy}
\label{app:hierarchy}

\begin{proposition}[Prefix extension requires negation in Datalog,
  restated]
  Standard encodings of prefix extension as a Datalog program over
  set-inclusion facts require negation.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  Encode a sequence of length $n$ as a set of position-value facts
  $\{\mathit{pos}(1, v_1), \mathit{pos}(2, v_2), \ldots,
  \mathit{pos}(n, v_n)\}$.
  Under set inclusion, the fact set can only grow.
  In particular, nothing prevents two facts $\mathit{pos}(i, v)$ and
  $\mathit{pos}(i, v')$ with $v \neq v'$ from coexisting---a
  ``fork'' at position $i$.
  Prefix extension requires at most one value per position (the
  sequence is a function from positions to values).
  Enforcing this uniqueness constraint requires detecting the absence
  of a conflicting fact: ``there is no $\mathit{pos}(i, v')$ with
  $v' \neq v$.''
  This is a negation-as-failure check, expressible in stratified
  Datalog only via negation.
  Hence such encodings of prefix-extension semantics require
  at least one non-monotone (negated) subgoal.
\end{proof}

\medskip
\noindent
The subsumption extends beyond the base CALM theorem to the full
hierarchy of Ameloot, Ketsman, Neven, and Zinn~\cite{ameloot2015weaker}
and to Baccaert and Ketsman's generalized
CALM~\cite{baccaert2026spectrum}.

\begin{proposition}[Subsumption of $N_3$-coordination-freedom]
  \label{prop:n3}
  Under the transducer instantiation (Definition~\ref{def:transducer-inst})
  with $\mathcal{A}$ restricted to histories in which every node's
  system relation contains the full set of node identifiers from the
  start, a \emph{domain-independent} query $Q$ is
  $N_3$-coordination-free in the sense of
  Ameloot et al.~\cite{ameloot2015weaker} iff $\Spec_Q$ is monotone.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
  The restriction on $\mathcal{A}$ ensures that every admissible history
  begins with each node knowing all identifiers---the defining property
  of model $N_3$.
  Ameloot et al.~\cite{ameloot2015weaker} (Theorem~20) characterize
  $N_3$-coordination-free queries as exactly the domain-independent
  monotone queries.
  Under our instantiation, domain-independence is inherited from the
  query (it is a property of $Q$, not of the history space), and
  monotonicity of $Q$ coincides with monotonicity of $\Spec_Q$ by the
  same argument as Theorem~\ref{thm:subsumption}.
  The domain-independence requirement is necessary: queries that use
  the $\mathsf{All}$ relation (e.g., ``count the number of nodes'')
  are $N_3$-coordination-free but not monotone in the input---they
  exploit the known-membership assumption rather than input growth.
  Such queries are coordination-free \emph{given} the membership
  knowledge, which our framework captures by restricting $\mathcal{A}$
  rather than by relaxing monotonicity.
\end{proof}

\noindent
The $N_1$ and $N_2$ instantiations are analogous, restricting
$\mathcal{A}$ to histories consistent with the corresponding
information model (no system relations, or own-identifier only).
Baccaert and Ketsman's $\mathbf{C}$-monotonicity is subsumed
identically: restrict $\mathcal{A}$ to histories consistent with
configuration constraint $\mathbf{C}$.
