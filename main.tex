\begin{abstract}
  When is coordination \emph{intrinsically required} by a distributed
  specification?
  The CALM theorem answers this for monotone Datalog programs: coordination-free
  distributed evaluation coincides with syntactic monotonicity.
  But CALM is tied to a specific computational model (relational transducers)
  and a specific notion of input growth (set inclusion over facts).
  We give a characterization that applies to \emph{any} specification over
  Lamport histories with \emph{any} refinement order on outcomes.

  The \emph{Coordination Criterion} states that a specification admits a
  coordination-free implementation if and only if its observable outcomes are
  \emph{future-monotone}: as histories grow causally, admissible outcomes refine
  rather than contradict earlier observations.
  We prove that this criterion strictly generalizes CALM: there exist
  specifications that CALM classifies as requiring coordination but that the
  Coordination Criterion correctly identifies as coordination-free.
  In particular, a non-monotone program wrapped with coordination (e.g., a
  Paxos-ordered service) produces future-monotone observable outputs---a fact
  invisible to syntactic monotonicity analysis but captured by our semantic
  criterion.

  We instantiate the framework on specifications central to data management:
  transactional isolation levels, invariant confluence, CRDTs, and replicated
  objects under various consistency models.
  In each case, the same semantic test---future-monotonicity---recovers known
  coordination boundaries and provides a uniform explanation for why they arise.
\end{abstract}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ================================================================

\paragraph{CALM and relational transducers.}
The CALM theorem, conjectured by Hellerstein~\cite{hellerstein2010declarative}
and proved by Ameloot et al.~\cite{ameloot2013relational}, establishes that
monotone queries admit coordination-free distributed evaluation in the
relational transducer model.
Our Generalized CALM theorem subsumes this: relational transducers with
set-inclusion order on output facts are one instantiation of our framework
(Section~\ref{sec:beyond-calm}).
The key advance is that our criterion operates on \emph{observable output
semantics} rather than program syntax, enabling verification that
coordination correctly discharges a non-monotone specification---a
capability absent from the transducer-based analysis.

\paragraph{Baccaert and Ketsman: coordination spectrum.}
Baccaert and Ketsman~\cite{baccaert2023dcbq,baccaert2026spectrum} generalize
CALM in a complementary direction: they extend the transducer model to
non-deterministic ``behaviors'' (distributed instances $\to$ distributed
instances) and parameterize the system relations available to nodes, yielding
a coordination spectrum based on shared information.
Their generalization stays \emph{within} the transducer computational model
but varies what information nodes possess.
Ours leaves the transducer model entirely: we characterize coordination
requirements from the specification alone, independent of any computational
model.
The two generalizations are orthogonal:
their spectrum classifies problems by \emph{information requirements}
(how much shared state is needed);
ours classifies specifications by \emph{commitment structure}
(whether observable outcomes are future-monotone under a given refinement
order).
Crucially, their analysis---like the original CALM---operates on program
text and cannot recognize that a non-monotone program wrapped with
coordination produces future-monotone outputs (our Beyond CALM result,
Section~\ref{sec:beyond-calm}).

\paragraph{Li and Lee: replicated-object coordination-freedom.}
Li and Lee~\cite{li2025coordinationfree} develop a CALM-style equivalence
in a replicated-object architecture, characterizing coordination-freedom
in terms of replica consistency under growing input sets.
Their model assumes replication and merge semantics as primitives.
Our framework treats replication as one instance rather than an assumption:
the future relation is causal (via happens-before), not set-theoretic,
and most of our applications (isolation levels, agreement, snapshots)
do not concern replica consistency.
